I wasn’t sure exactly what I learned at CUAR but when I took the time to reflect, it was a formula that Pastor Chris Lee (speaker) left with us:

Spiritual Growth=(Willingness to Learn) * (Willingness to Change)

I realise that often I’m really willing to learn, but not exactly willing to change much. I tend to be quite complacent or stubborn in terms of liking to stay put and being in control (ie. changes or uncertainties w my future, etc. often give me insecurities), which might explain why I haven’t matured much w/in the last year. Pastor Chris explained that even if one factor is only 1% and say the other is say 10%, you’ll still get 10% of growth, but if one factor is lacking (0), 10*0 is still 0, so you’ll experience no effects in your spiritual walk with God. Makes perfect sense now that I see the analogy visually. I also need to be more passionate for Christ.

Addtionally, I was once again reminded of my commitment to reach out to the non-Christians around me wherever God placed me. I remember last term in my Prayer Duo (w Vonnekins), I had a notion that whether I was in Ottawa or in Toronto, I was responsible to reach out to certain individuals and live a living testimony of God’s love. I’m not entirely sure that I’ve done my part just yet…tho’ I pray that my lifestyle will reflect the joys of being a Christian :).

Another thought that occured to me was the word “Retreat” itself. The word reminded me that the weekend is supposed to be a “Retreat from the distractions of everyday life, and a retreat unto God”…

Since the move, I’ve been pretty much bored. Since going to the library I finished 3 H.P books already, and The Poisonwood Bible. Now that i’ve read the books, I think the movie is more interesting to watch, tho a tad bit long. Back when HP was overly-hyped, there was a controversy (and still is, though not as much) about the very nature of the books in the Christian circle. I see the bad (highly influential w theme/nature of books), and I see the good (friendships, good vs evil) in the books. But if JK Rowling gets much criticism for what she writes, why didn’t other children’s lit authors such as RL Stine (eg. Goosebumps), Christopher Pike, Bruce Coville (eg. Jeremy Thatcher, Dragon Hatcher) , Ruth Chew (eg. Second-hand Magic), and even Roald Dahl, get equally criticised? Even Dahl’s Matilda was made into a movie and she had special powers. These (cept Pike and Stine–that was more high school) were some my favourite children authors. Any books that had to do with ghosts and the supernatural, mystery, and suspense I’d be captivated. I didn’t see anyone warning me about the dangerous powers. However, I see a point in warning a child with a wild imagination, such as mine, to be careful about what one reads as books influence ones thinking quite easily as well and to keep an open eye before reading and discussing with a parent or somethng after is prolly always better. Related thought: CS Lewis’ The Chronicles of Narnia also has good and bad witches within Narnia–keeping in mind that his books are of Christian allegory, how much difference does that make?

Onto Barbara Kingsolver’s The Poisonwood Bible, I figured out why SilQ doesn’t like and why it’s an Oprah book choice. I read Paradise Lost by Toni Morrison for Women’s Lit one year and that was also an Oprah pick. I didn’t like it after reading it. Both books are women-oriented–how the women are oppressed in all sorts of ways (in spirit, emotionally, etc.) and how each woman manage to escape and change (or not) at the end. The plot of The Poisionwood Bible moves quite slowly but by the end of the book, you see how each character develops or remains the same. Tho’ this book kinda gives a negative impression of missionary families. The father is somewhat a hypocrite and is more concerned about preaching the words of the Bible than living his life reflective of the Bible. I hope there are less missionary fathers like that out there though the adjusting to a new place for the MKs, etc. seems very realistic.

This time around, the worship team led (harmonizable! :P) songs from a variety of groups instead of only fron Hillsongs/Vineyard. Closing my eyes, I could almost imagine the sounds of heaven: it will be 100 and more times fuller than what it sounded like that night. Bumped into Andrew Fung, one of the Spice Girls in the CCF Lifesong in 98. Wow, I didn’t realise that he grew up at the Met.

After, we headed to Elgin Street Diner on … Elgin Street! heheh. Marcy and I each had a slice of cheesecake and the rest of the table seemed to have breakfasts. They’re not too bad in terms of being low class because they have 2-ply toilet paper at least. Jeff was the “lap-sap toong” (garbage can) for the night because he cleared a plate of pancakes from Viv b/c she couldn’t finish it, and Matt’s leftovers as well–quite an amusing sight :P. Gravy sauntered in midway and we ended up celebrating his bday at midnight there with an apple pie :)–so he’s an old geezer now; 24 years old!

Sunday service at OCAC was aite. Guest Speaker from some church in TO (ManJon Wong?) spoke on his upcming trip to Amsterdam. SS was okay though I don’t have a high tolerance for video lecturers; reminds me of TV evangelists, and RC Sproul–they put me to sleep and I get really restless. I don’t know what to do this term, there doesn’t seem to be a good variety of classes to choose from. Maybe I’ll got to C.L.A.S.S. 201 since I have yet to attend that one (been to BAC’s 101, OCAC’s 301 and some of 401).

After SS, I quickly left to make it for my cousin’s first communion. Très bizarre! We went to St. Marie’s on Innes Road and it was a pure 100% French-speaking Catholic church. Firstly, I have never been to a mass or a service at a RC church, and secondly, I cannot understand French. Thus, I was not familiar in anything. I walk in and I think the only two Chinese ppl there were me and my uncle. I look around and it looks soooo contemporary. The ceilings are like gymnasiums and the colours are vibrant. There is no organ and the choir is comprised of vocalists and instruments: drums (snare mostly), flute, guitars(?). I didn’t know any of the “hymns” they sang, which were unsurprisingly easy to learn. Not that many in the congregation were singing. One of them was to the tune of “Michael, row your bow ashore, Hallelujah” and I was bewildered to hear them play a children’s song; I never got the meaning of that song, even as a child. The verse on the wall paraphrased Matthew 5:13-14 in French quite well: You are the salt of the earth; You are the light of the world. The 2 phrases look and sound very artistic for some reason in French.

The priest/Father was not as boring as I preconceived him to be. He was quite lively and used Q&A to get the congregation to interact with them. It’s rare that a preacher steps down from the pulpit and walk through the aisles: reminds me of Keeping the Faith somehow. Did I understand him? Hmm, not really. I think I only recognised John 14:1-4. And then there was communion. Everyone lined up to get the bread and the optional “cup”. Once it was your turn, you’d cupping your hand out as if you’re beggin’ and after hearing the usher say, “God’s grace” or something along those lines (that was surprisingly in English or maybe I just heard wrong), you’d say “Amen” and take the bread. The person would then take the bread and dip it in the cup or drink from the cup. Gross, sharing germs! My cousins just took it and ate it like a cracker for fun of it. I don’t think they know the meaning of the bread and cup. At first, I wanted to opt out, but then figured that since I’ve taken communion before … At the very end of the service, the Father explained the whole cross motion thing but because it was in French, I didn’t catch much of it. Maybe Rice can explain it to me one day.

Spent dinner over at my aunt’s and some time w my cousins; did some beading craft w Camille. My aunt cooked up a really great Middle Eastern meal once again :). My uncle flew to the States for a month so she’s got a handful of kids to look after! I will head over to their place again on Friday to tutor Max in Chinese coz he’s got an Chinese-school exam on Saturday. I think it’s in Mandarin. Hehe, that should be fun! 🙂
