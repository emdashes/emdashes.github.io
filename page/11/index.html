Month six: solids, cloth diapers, and teething

Solids Month six brings a major change in Little Bean‚Äôs daily routine (and yes, routine is important; it makes or breaks a good day): the introduction of ‚Äúsolid‚Äù food. At her six month check-up, the doctor asked if she had started to sit tripod-style (yes), transfer objects from one hand to another (kind of?), and put everything in her mouth (doctor said that other parents would be envious that she only puts her fingers in her mouth). While her weight is still on the lower percentile of the graph, her length is back to being average, but maybe we have a late bloomer, because a few days later, she decided to gum everything. Ah, one of the signs that she‚Äôs ready for solids! We started her a week late, which happened to coincide with Chinese New Year, and while she immediately took a liking to rice cereal (store-bought: Baby Gourmet which only has soy lecithin in addition to fortified nutrients, compared to other commercial rice cereals that have ingredients such as vanilla and other random additives), her face told us that she wasn‚Äôt expecting mashed avocado in place of it, when we introduced that to her next. However, a consequence of a new diet means stinkier and more explosive diapers.

Cloth diapers

We wouldn‚Äôt have tried using cloth diapers had our friends not lent us some Bum Genius diapers. At first, the diapers would leak but I soon realized it was because she was still too small for them, but the diapers have advanced since I last remembered them in my childhood days. Gone are the muslin cloths and safety pins, and in their place are ‚Äúhook and loop‚Äù closures (aka velcro), diaper inserts, liners, and material that wicks (PUL).¬† We haven‚Äôt used them out yet, as they‚Äôre not as convenient as disposables, but I have the sense that the cloth diapers are more breathable on her bum. It helps to have friends who also use cloth diapers as I‚Äôve come across some tips from them and on the internet, in no particular order:
  • Breastfed babies diapers are the easiest to manage, as you can just throw everything in the wash, without needing to rinse or flush.
  • Because the diapers are now very absorbent, I sometimes can‚Äôt smell it, but the guideline of changing her every 2-3 hours is a good window, as the diaper is almost always wet within that timeframe.
  • Stains that still remain after a wash can disappear with sun exposure, even if it has gone through the dryer.¬† Yes, something magical happens with UV rays and removing stains! Now I understand why people dry their laundry outdoors, in addition to getting a nice fresh breeze through them. However, with the cold and snow, I‚Äôve resorted putting them next to the window, and over a course of a few days, sure enough, the stains fade! However, a couple of friends also recommend using buncha farmers all-natural stain remover, so I will try that next time and the residual stains from her explosion on her clothes too.
  • My friend suggested a more economical way to catch poop in the diaper, so to save on having to rinse it before washing: line a soft paper towel between the diaper and babe‚Äôs bum ‚Äì or better yet, Charmin extra strong supposedly does the trick too.

Teething

In other news, Little Bean IS teething ‚Äì no teeth is poking out yet, but I put my finger in her mouth the other night, and felt something sharp on her left. She had woken up wailing and the usual tricks to lull her back to sleep consistently failed until another friend suggested using baby Advil to help her with the pain. Well, we had baby Tylenol instead, and it sure is effective; she went back to sleep within 15 minutes!

Kefir, and breast milk

breast milk?‚Äù

Kefir

After a recent trip to the farm, the hosts gave me some kefir grains to try cultivating, when I expressed some remote interest and enthusiasm that they‚Äôve been making homemade kefir. Grace first introduced me to store-bought effervescent kefir, served with a homemade rhubarb compote, and I returned to it recently when I saw it for 50% off at the grocery store. Then, I bought the non-effervescent one for regular price (which goes for $3.5/946ml = $very pricey in my books), when I read that kefir is a yogurt-like drink that has three major things going for it:
  1. It is low in lactose (for the [somewhat] lactose-intolerant, like myself)
  2. It is high in calcium (1 serving = 30%)
  3. It is high in probiotics, thought to contain more than yogurt itself

Homemade Kefir

When sampling their homemade version, more things came to mind:
  1. This tastes nothing like the store-bought variety; as Stephanie succinctly put it, it has strong flavours of yogurt+beer
  2. This smells nothing like the store-bought variety; it smells like yeast
  3. Milk can be ‚Äústored‚Äù in room temperature? (I guess with kefir, it can?)
  4. What? They bought the kefir grains from kijiji? I didn‚Äôt realize that a) they were that tech-saavy (!), and b) shady-much?
  5. This brought me back to a faint childhood memory: I had a fizzy strawberry milk drink when I was in kindergarten at a classmate‚Äôs house. I had always wondered if it was just an East Indian drink consisting of sprite+strawberry milk, but maybe it‚Äôs a version of kefir?

Observations

As I experimented with kefir this week, I came across these findings:
  1. Store-bought kefir doesn‚Äôt smell like homemade kefir because it doesn‚Äôt contain yeast
  2. The smell of yeast in homemade kefir is stronger than it should taste (should taste more sour than yeasty)
  3. There is a very brief window of when kefir is ‚Äòready‚Äô before it turns into kefir curds and whey. When it‚Äôs ‚Äòready‚Äô, it has a milder flavour (less beer-like) and has a more similar consistency to store-bought kefir, which is looking to be around the 12 hour mark with my proportions of milk to grain and the surrounding environment.
  4. My sudden intake of kefir may or may not have affected the change in smell of breastfed baby‚Äôs bowel movements (yeastier! *tmi, i know)
  5. As I tended to this micro-organism/bacterial colony, I couldn‚Äôt help but think that cultivating kefir grains is similar to cultivating our seed of faith ‚Äì our relationship with God; you have to tend to it, for it to grow and as a result, multiply.

Comparisons

So, while I wasn‚Äôt giving some breast milk to my friend, I suppose kefir is similar to breast milk, in that:
  • they both contain plenty of nutrients
  • both can differ in taste and texture, depending on the stage it‚Äôs at (or time of day)
  • and both are sometimes referred to as ‚Äòliquid gold‚Äô (see kefir vs. breast milk)
I tend to have a black thumb when it comes to plants, so we‚Äôll see if they will survive for an extended time. If you‚Äôre brave to try the end product, let me know and I‚Äôd be happy to share. * In the community: a local article about kefir (and other fermented products)]]>
