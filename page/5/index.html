    Tick talk: when it's not a skin tag

    Photo courtesy of my mother-in-law. After finally realizing what it was, it looks like it has burrowed into her head[/caption] Around 6:30 AM one morning, Bean woke up and didn’t go back to sleep but was content to lie in bed with me. At some point, she told me that something was bothering her at the back of her head (her one-worded summary: “ow”), near the nape of her neck. In my sleepy haze, I parted her hair and took a peek. A skin-coloured lump, almost the size of my pinky fingernail, puzzled and troubled me so I tried to get a closer look with a tissue because I didn’t want to touch it because it looked like it had hair follicles, which also sort of resembled short insect-like legs. So, trying to hide my “heebies jeebies”[ref]the “heebie jeebies” accurately sums how I feel, as used by one forum poster with his experience[/ref], I asked her if she wanted Daddy or Grandma to come in to help me, and she said Grandma, so I went to get a second opinion. Grandma later told me she took a video and some photos and sent it to a doctor friend of hers, who admitted he didn’t know what it was, perhaps an insect bite or an infection with a sac. We made an appointment with our family doctor, and I started googling and looking for images of skin tags that matched what I had seen – nothing that looked that size, even though the description seemed to match. At the doctor’s office, the nurse and the doctor took a look and confirmed that it was a skin tag. Her explanation was in line with what I had read online: that they’re benign, often made of the same cells and components of your skin, so you will see things like hair follicles, and that this one had a stalk. I couldn’t figure out how I missed it (since I comb her hair daily), but I asked how long it could have been there for, and she explained that it could have been there for awhile but was hard to spot because it wasn’t bothering Bean. She told us that it look like it was going to auto-amputate because the stalk end/base was starting to turn bluish, so we had a few options:

    1. Leave it be, and see how long it would take. Usually for smaller skin tags, it takes 2-3 days, but this one could be slower because of its size.
    2. Use liquid nitrogen to burn it off. I asked how the pain would be compared to a needle, and she said it would probably be the same intensity but longer.
    3. Something similar to #2 but with local freezing. We opted to go with this.
    [caption id="attachment_5269" align="alignright" width="225"] Photo courtesy of our doctor[/caption] For whatever reason, she decided to take a second look, and this time, IT MOVED. I appreciate that she didn’t freak out, because I would have – and certainly did inside. Instead, she calmly said that she was going outside to get a second doctor’s opinion and help. The second doctor was a lot more certain that it was a tick and they partnered to quickly remove it with a pair of tweezers; it was still very much alive in the urine sample jar they put it in, so we assume that every bit came out (thus reducing Lyme disease, if at risk) – the second doctor must have really wanted to make sure for he must have pulled extra skin and hair off, because Bean started crying even more and I was handed a piece of gauze to apply pressure to stop the bleeding[ref]a week later, the spot looks raised with a dark scab, and it must feel tender because it still bothers her[/ref]. Google helped identify the tick as a dog tick, which isn’t as great of a threat as the black-legged ticks, but they jarred it anyway to be sent for analysis. She called a contact to see if we needed to start antibiotic treatment, as their guidelines only offered advice to those over 8 years of age. Later, she also followed up to tell us that Public Health would call if they found something of concern and texted us some close-up photos. We don’t have a dog, but we did go to the urban natural area the day before, and that’s likely where we picked it up. I also appreciated that she apologized and retracted her first diagnosis; she had seen ticks before, but none this size[ref]I don’t blame her – Google tells me that it was an adult female in its partially fed state so it would be bigger[/ref]. She’s a family doctor, so I can’t expect her to know everything, even though we hope they know what ails us. She recognized that even though I had made comments about characteristics reminding me of an insect, it was likely that everyone’s quick glance assumptions as a skin tag threw everyone off. [caption id="attachment_5267" align="alignright" width="300"] Looking from the outside of the jar – doesn’t look as big as the close up, nor as creepy as when it was on her head.[/caption] I like being outdoors. I enjoy aspects of camping, canoeing, and especially hiking; I remember my dad would take us on walks and bike rides around trails when we were much younger. In all my years doing activities outdoors, I would have never imagined having to deal with this. Thankfully, I think this experience was more traumatic for me than it was for Bean, since she didn’t see what was bothering her, despite some tears and pain. Thank God that Bean noticed it, and He answered our prayers, giving wisdom to our doctor who for reason decided to do a second  look – because who knows if we would’ve found it otherwise. Plus, I am really glad that I didn’t have to remove it myself.]]>

    Waterloo Region Waste Management – Open House

    [caption id="attachment_5222" align="alignright" width="300"] Cubes of compacted recycled cans[/caption] After a morning of building most of the little library, we rushed to the waste management site. I had signed us up to go because of Bean’s interest in garbage trucks and buses – a school bus was to take us on a tour of the site, with pauses at the current landfill, and a stop at the recycling centre. If their goal of open houses is to send an environmental message home, I think it achieved it with our family; it probably ended up being the most educational for us, the parents. Here’s what I learned:
    • The (now world-wide) blue box recycling program started in Kitchener
    • The waste management site “hires” two hawks to send seagulls away a few times a week
    • Mount Trashmore, our local popular tobogganing hill, was discontinued as a landfill in the ’70s
    • Landfills that are at max capacity are capped with ceramic before being topped with soil and grass
    • Items you throw in the garbage that you know are biodegradable or claims to biodegrade in X days won’t actually ever if it ends up in the landfill because the environment isn’t right for it; it actually gets packed down (sort of WALL-E-like), so it lacks oxygen and organisms that break it down
    • A tarp-like sheet goes over every layer of garbage to keep it from blowing away and fumes and moisture are sent through pipes, where the methane gas is collected and converted into energy; I didn’t see any burning pipes anywhere, nor did it smell nearly as bad as the one we used to drive back next to Highway 1 back home before it got converted to a driving range
    You could say that we unknowingly took part in some Earth Day weekend activities, with a specific focus on recycling / reusing (books in the community instead of the garbage, and learning about recycling operations in our city). It felt like a long day, but it wasn’t even mid-afternoon yet by the time we finished! While I do enjoy doing our regular stops, after this past Saturday’s activities together as a family, I find it’s worth waking up early if it means doing something different. A few months ago, someone from the a local shop came to the midwives’ new baby time to chat about cloth diapering and babywearing. Like the mommy-jargon, there’s plenty of diapering lingo too, like “stink[ref]the way the diapers smell if it’s saturated or has residue in it, even after washing – though I still don’t know what ‘barnyard stink’ is – probably a good thing[/ref] “, “strip[ref]to clean out any residue in attempts to solve a smell or repelling/leaking problem – I’ve been using unconcentrated blue dawn from the dollar store[/ref]”, “pockets” or “prefolds”[ref]type of cloth diapers[/ref]. After seeing her demonstrate a few diapering options, I think if I had gone into cloth diapering from scratch[ref]then again, if we weren’t passed down a set, we probably would’ve only gone with disposables[/ref] and did some research, I would have ended up with prefolds because it seems the most economic and you don’t have to toss the diaper cover in the wash after every change (only the inside cloth needs to be laundered), especially if it isn’t soiled or wet, a system similar to what my mom used with us when we were younger [ref]hers would likely have been classified as flats[/ref] (I still use her diapers cloths as burp cloths and to absorb leaking breastmilk). However, we were passed down a set of Bum Genius hook and loop closure (i.e. Velcro but I think that name is trademarked) diapers with inserts, so we’ve stuck with the same type of diapers in our stash – which probably is easier to use for dad and when there is help. I didn’t even know these were considered pocket diapers until much later. However, I’ve moved away from stocking velcro-based diapers, because they stick to each other and they seem to wear out faster. Maybe I am not as meticulous when it comes to taking care of our stuff, but now they are well-used and well-loved in comparison to looking like-new when we first received them (they have been through one child prior). Because the elastics in the legs are also getting more and more relaxed, I’ve designated these to our toddler and ended buying more diapers to add to my collection, but they’re not brand-name diapers and they’re all snap-based instead of velcro. They seem harder to put on and take off, but they don’t seem to wear out as quickly either. I have also added bamboo diapers and inserts into my collection, and while they seem more soft and absorbent than my microfleece diapers and inserts, stains seem harder to wash out from natural fibers than synthetics. Because we’re using the bamboo diapers with our baby, I’m new to EBF stains that turn blue and green after a wash/dry cycle – can’t wait for some sunny weather to see if sunlight will help. Thankfully stains don’t affect function, just the aesthetics. Since I didn’t spend more than $5 per diaper-insert set, I’m also not too concerned about needing to sell them again after we no longer use them so the stains aren’t a big deal.]]>
