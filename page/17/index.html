Croatia

In September, we went on another Mediterranean cruise, this time beginning and ending in Venice with the company of my parents and my brother.

Because of time restrictions, we chose a 7-day cruise with the Norwegian Jade (NCL). Compared to the Carnival Dream, the Jade was a lot smaller, much simpler, and we had less activities to choose from to pass time while on board/at sea. NCL, however, has better live shows (entertainment) than the Dream and it allows you to have a much more flexible schedule, so you can dine when you want and where you want, with very little restrictions on the dress code.

We got chatting with a few of the crew members on board, and found out that many of them come from less-wealthy countries, leave their families behind and work on board for over 9 months, with long hours each day. One 20 year old young man started telling us about his family and he teared up just thinking that he would see them in 2 months.

Once again, Croatia was one of my favourite ports that we stopped at ‚Äì both Dubrovnik (above) and Split (below). We had beautiful weather, with blue skies and sunny days, despite it being very hot (hotter than our last trip). NCL also tendered at the port, so we didn‚Äôt have to walk half an hour before reaching the old town, which was a great bonus!

Split is probably known for their abundance of lavender; both cities sell potpurri packets filled with lavender, but it was in Split that we came across plants that were very fragrant ‚Äì turns out they were fresh lavender plants!

It was also in Split that we happened upon a rather deserted restaurant called Luna Cafe that looked rather pricey. However, we all tried the local beer and the seafood was probably the freshest that I had in a very long while, and the price was worth it! j.w enjoyed a kilogramof mussels (~$15?), which wasn‚Äôt fishy at all, and my mother and I split a whole pan-fried fish seasoned with some sea salt ($30).Ôªø

UX Week: Seductive Interaction Design Workshop

The business goals (outcome) should align with user goals (what people do), but Anderson stressed that knowing how humans behave or what motivates one, should not be used in manipulation coercion. We need to think about what poeple need to do in order for your business (goal) to be successful. His advice? Start with functionality, then build out the fun. Sure, one could look to gaming techniques, but instead of motiviating human behaviour through game mechanics (a concept known as gamification), motivate through psychology. The first often focus on extrinsic rewards are often short-lived, but the latter focuses on instrinsic motiviation. So what motivates human behaviour? He shared some design ideas on how to do this, such as:

  • Recognition with one‚Äôs own name, human faces
  • The desire to complete an incomplete set
  • Desire for self-expression: customizing, creating own profile, self goals and association with self

From UX Week 2011
I have high expectations of UX Week as I chose to attend particular UX ‚Äì user experience event/conference (from a selection of many) based on recommendations from people. But, I think I tried to compare it too much to Interactions (an IxD conference), which seems to have more of a practical focus, and also more my area of interest. So far, UX Week feels more like TED talks for smart UX people, which I am not. Some of the demos, like the ones by 18 year olds blew me away (in a good way) ‚Äì companies should be hiring these students! At times, it also felt like Adaptive Path case studies or success stories instead of impartial talks. It‚Äôs more like going to university than college: you don‚Äôt get practical hands-on experience unless you attend the workshops; instead, the talks are rather abstract and somehow you manage to extract principles around the various topics on politics, biology (genetics), business (economics), and mathematics (statistics, algorithms, geometry). And, like school, you see and meet new faces and hope to make friends in your classes/program ‚Äì some people know each other from before, or are attending together, but others are like me, attending for the first time and the only one representing their company. Nice touches: moleskin notebooks to jot down interesting notes and pre-washed alumnimum water bottles to make sure you‚Äôre hydrated throughout the conference. And, they take you to field trips: to the club and to the California Academy of Sciences Night Life, not to mentioned sponsored lunches (although some food ran out by the time I got to the front of the line), and drinks (alcholic and non-alcoholic).
