A week in Vancouver

Recently, we took a two week vacation from our regular routine. The few weeks leading to it crescendoed into a flurry of activities: wrapping up current work, ensuring that everything would go smoothly for KJ’s wedding, planning the trip and finding accomodations, and packing. And, what would a true wedding be without a few hiccups and last minute scrambles? It certainly made for a memorable one: many firsts, such as seeing a bride ask the officiating pastor if her and her groom could kiss now, and returning from the recession to sign the registry (oops). I was still able to squeeze in some meaningful conversations, connect with some familiar faces again at the wedding, and hearing sad news from the BAC family (prayers needed!).

While Fall would be my favourite time of the year in Ontario (because of the beautiful colours of the changing leaves), Spring is the perfect time of the year to visit Vancouver / the West Coast; daffodils and tulips are blooming, and the trees are blossoming everywhere! I took j.w to my two favourite spots when it’s this time of the year: Burnaby Mountain and Queen Elizabeth Park.

And what is a trip to Vancouver without some good food? Tunger and Mich, the go-to couple for food, hehe. And of course, to get j.w’s sushi fix! Our menu for the week consisted of the following:

  1. Wednesday
    Late meal: some random restaurant to eat as I was hungry from the flight. It was a first from what I remember: perserved duck egg with lean pork congee, but with dried oysters, and it was good. 
  2. Thursday
    Lunch: my mom took me to a (newish) Taiwanese restaurant that is conveniently close to home. Turns out, she was right about bumping into my uncle there; he often goes for lunch but which is no wonder, as their portion sizes are huge = great value! (and pretty good too). Had a McD’s snack size $2 smoothie for dessert (took advantage of their promotion).
    Dinner: a very fulfilling (and cleansing) wedding rehearsal dinner at Dai Tong (大同﻿). We had a very lovely purple yam soup dessert that looked like dimetapp but tasted like the gingery 湯圓﻿ soup – another first for me!
  3. Friday
    Lunch: Mich and Tunger told me about Hi Genki, a Japanese restaurant that serves home-style cooked dishes in a Japanese retirement home / cultural centre in Burnaby. Since my mom doesn’t like raw fish, I suggested that we should try their dishes. However, I feel that you need to know what to order, as the dishes can be a hit or miss (one hit was a herbal broth like fish stew).
    Dinner: My brother recommended Bamboo Grove, a Chinese restaurant that’s not for the budget-friendly folks. In fact, they don’t even list prices on some of their menu items, like the Pork Stomach and Gingko Long Boiled Soup” 老火 豬 肚 湯﻿ (btw, the small is HUGE – more than enough to feed 4 of us). Okay, that doesn’t sound very appealing, and it might taste pretty foul if some restaurants don’t do a proper job of cleaning, quite offal, but there wasn’t nothing off about this one. Actually, I was surprised to find that it was similar to some of the soups that my father-in-law makes!
  4. Saturday
    Lunch: I didn’t really eat a lunch that day, but the Tai family generously fed me some of their delicious crispy pork (whole piglet) that the groom’s family had delivered first thing in the morning. Then, Mich took us to Fausborg, a boulangerie bakery cafe that had prices of Paris, and had some good almond croissants and coffee! (though cafe in Italy still ranks higher in my mind).
    Dinner: 10 course wedding banquet at Vancouver’s famous Sun Sui Wah. Wasn’t sure what it was (stress or overtired maybe?) that I couldn’t stomach too much and by the time the evening came to a close, my stomach was burning up in pain. Alas.
    While I was occupied, j.w had his fill of sushi for lunch, and crab poutine and some other delicious food at Stevenson for dinner and 豆腐花﻿, one of his favourite desserts, in Richmond. I’m glad that She/R took him to Stevenson, as it was on my itinerary of taking him there someday (I personally don’t remember what it looks like).
  5. Sunday
    Lunch: with Mich and Tunger, She, Becks and Cire, and Waiki’s brother. I found it tremendously amusing to hear Cire comment about the likeness of mannerisms in my sister and I – that’s probably why Tunger thought I was her to begin with once upon a time. Lunch was followed by a large mango tapoica drink at Crystal Mall for $3.75, but I think I like 8 Juice more at Aberdeen Centre for their mango pomelo drink.
    Dinner: a home cooked meal with fresh salmon served two ways: cedar plank smoked BBQ and fried with black bean and garlic. and prawns, both from Stevenson.
  6. Monday
    Lunch: with my grandparents at Lin’s, a place known for its 小籠包﻿,  steamed pork dumplings with its juice wrapped in thin, delicate skin. I also enjoyed their 紅油﻿炒﻿手﻿ (but was thirsty after)
    Dinner:﻿$1 oysters at Monk McQueen’s, along with a pick of fish tacos for my main. I’d say that it isn’t really worth it at regular price without a BOGO free entertainment book coupon, but the $1 oysters were a deal at half price on Mondays and Tuesdays: not fishy or slimey.﻿
  7. Tuesday
    Lunch: we treked to Deer Garden in Richmond (a return from Christmas) to have their u-pick soup noodle. This time I was a bit smarter and split a bowl with my mom, and was still plenty full after! We also certainly got our fill of 老婆餅﻿ from Kam Do Bakery, but it was bought for later breakfasts at home.
    Dinner: met up with friends at a Korean sushi restaurant, a old funeral home in the past and finished the evening with a giant deep-friend ice cream ball from Hi Genki (and bumped into Mich as well!)
  8. A week later on Tuesday for dinner: large-portioned meals at the One, including super-sized slushies (found it still to be really sweet even at half sweet, and semi-artificial)
  9. Wednesday:
    Breakfast: My brother recommended an “authentic” HK breakfast cafe in Richmond called Lido for their 菠蘿油﻿ (pure slice of butter in between a freshly baked pineapple bun). I paired it with some 雪﻿菜肉﻿絲公仔麵.﻿﻿Turns out, it was the only breakfast place that I’ve been to in Richmond; Stephen Siu, a old CCFer had taken me there once when he was in town and had showed me the ACF office nearby, but I only had macaroni soup there that time.

 It’s no wonder why I am constantly hungry now.﻿

Things to do: Montreal+Ottawa

Ottawa:

  1. Parliament Hill (tours conducted in English or French – multiple daily) – be sure to go up to the clock tower (has a nice view of Ottawa) – Free (note: you will need to go through a security check but cameras area allowed on site). At night, the building might light up for a display and changing of the guard on certain days and times.
  2. Canadian Museum of Civilization (Gatineau)
  3. If you’re into Thai food, Siam Bistro at the other end of Somerset Ave (other direction than Chinatown) is a bit slow and a bit expensive, but I like their dishes.
  4. Seeing the locks and how they work at Rideau Canal is neat, but if you’ve been to Panama canal, it’s no big deal – my old notes say it’s beside Chateau Laurier (nice hotel – royalty stay there, use their restrooms inside to see what I mean)
  5. They have to try a Beavertail (near the Byward Market though also available in Old Montreal somewhere)
  6. Walk around the Byward Market
  7. Royal Canadian Mint – sign up for the tour and if you go on a weekday, you may be able to see them make the coins
  8. If you like airplanes, there’s also the Canadian Aviation and Space museum, but I don’t remember much of it

Montreal:

  1. Botanical Gardens – especially at night for the Lantern Festival display (must see if right season – in the Autumn)
  2. If you like penguins and animals, then the Biodome
  3. Montreal bagels: St. Viateur (there are a number of them, so you might want to try and look for the one that you can sit down if you don’t want to just do take-out) – look for the St. Viateur Bagel and Cafe sign (http://www.flickr.com/photos/aimeili/1547259044/in/set-72157602365174063/lightbox/)
  4. Schwartz’ (for Montreal smoked meat) and nearby: Juliet et Chocolat (for crepes)
  5. Walk around Old Montreal – if you get hungry around there and don’t want to eat in Chinatown, then eat at Chez Suzette’s for their savoury crepes (in Old Montreal)
  6. Chinatown for egg tarts (best at the corner / last shop on the street called Patisserie Harmonie– it’s 50 cents each and so good)and HK style cafe at La Legende Patisserie
  7. If you want poutine (which is icky in my mind but a typical French Canadian food), try it at La Banquise
  8. St. Joseph’s oratory and the lookout points at Mont Royal
  9. Jeremy’s church is Montreal Chinese Alliance Church
  10. Slightly expensive but good (and huge portions, perfect for sharing) brunch place after church: Le Petite Marche – it’s in the heart of the French community so it might be better if you knew some French
  11. If open, any Premiere Moisson bakery is great for dessert and pastries
Additional food stuff for Montreal:
  • Gourmet chocolates at (I went to Atwater Market so if you’re comfortable with French then you’ll be fine…I wasn’t but was still fine)
  • Coffee and beer is good in Montreal too. I just can’t remember the names of specifics.
  • there’s this mediterranean/lebanese seafood restaurant that we went to where we get to pick you own fish at the fish market and they fry it up for you! so freesh and the salads were great. but it’s a TAD on the pricy side. too bad I can’t remember what it was called. it may been: http://www.yelp.ca/biz/restaurant-la-sirene-de-la-mer-montreal

A Distant World

Eyes on Me‘ that I remember encountering the characters of Final Fantasy for the first time; I enjoyed watching the storyline, but I had no patience to play through it. Our friend, Carey, was the source for what’s new in the Canto community (like the movie 風雲 The Storm Riders) – or maybe it was just what he liked – and this song was one of them. It wasn’t long before that it made it into the binder at karaoke shops, as it was very easy to sing along. Actually, even though I haven’t played any of the games, I paid to watch Final Fantasy: the Spirits Within in the theatres. I remember thinking it didn’t have very much plot, but I thought the animation and the soundtrack were both quite good, despite it sounding depressing, so I actually ended up listening to the soundtrack multiple times afterwards. I also saw Final Fantasy: Advent Children, but I don’t remember much from it. I must have been exposed to more Final Fantasy without knowing it over the years, because at the Final Fantasy: Distant Worlds concert with KW Symphony, conducted by Arnie Roth, I recognized quite a number of pieces, including the one that’s been playing on the TV in our living room for the past few weeks (j.w is working through Final Fantasy XIII). If it’s any indication of how many hours he’s been playing it, as soon as the conductor said, “…Blinding Light from Final Fantasy XIII”, I nudged j.w and whispered, THAT’S THE SONG! He looked at me quizzically and then upon hearing the key notes after the intro, he gave me a look that said, “How did you know!?” I’m glad KW Symphony reduced the prices of those $80 tickets; that was a nice treat (though it wasn’t a full house which might explain why – so next time, I might look for last minute seats again). Even though it ended up to be $35 each for A seats (Mezzaine 3rd row center), I feel like we got a better deal than Toronto’s, especially since the one in Toronto is played by KW Symphony anyway! Granted, the composer Nobuo Uematsu wasn’t there nor did Arnie Roth play a Violin solo, unlike the Toronto performance, but I don’t feel like that justifies the difference (balcony seats in Toronto are now selling for $83.50). We got to hear previews from FFIX, but I especially liked the encore piece, One-Winged Angel (FFVII); the audience got to participate by being the chorus and singing Seph-i-roth! Watching the percussionists run around scrambling to be in position so that they can hit their beat in time was quite entertaining to watch, and hearing Susan Calloway sing ‘Eyes on Me’ brought it back full circle. So, I guess it was a pretty fun night out at the symphony again (just like Video Games Live and Star Trek). And, by saying that, it must make me sound like a huge nerd.]]>

A Titanic tragedy

This year marks the 100th anniversary of the sinking of the Titanic, with James Cameron re-releasing his epic movie, Titanic in 3D [really? who wants to sit through another 3 hours and see the poor guy falling into the propeller? ]. Last year, we visited the Titanic: the artifact exhibit at the Museum at the end of its run, and learned a few things about that voyage including the prices to get on that ship unexpectedly high, the pristine condition of some of the ship’s artifacts (like dining room plates), and that strangely enough, J. Dawson wasn’t a listed passenger on the ship. I thought it strange because J. Dawson’s grave marker is in one of Halifax’s cemetery where they buried some of the victims of that tragedy. After not seeing that name on the passenger list (that they had pasted to the wall), I inquired the Museum staff about the missing person, to which they told me was a fictious name and person, and that if I recalled correctly (from the film, I gather), he was a stowaway. Yet, some research on the Internet seems to suggest that he was a real person, a member of the crew. According to Wikipedia, the ship had more than enough lifeboats as per regulations, but didn’t have enough to carry all its passenger and crew. Yet, not every lifeboat was launched full, and most of the passengers perished. Of the stories told, it was women and children who were given first priority – perhaps what we’d call today, an act of chivalry. I had wanted to blog about our trip to the Titanic exhibit months ago, but hadn’t gotten around to it, until now. Coincidentally, about 100 years later, another tragedy strikes, what many describe as modern-day Titanic. Costa Concordia, one of the cruise line’s biggest ship hits a rock and listed. Despite half the lifeboats not being able to launch, many passengers were saved or swam to shore. The media reports in the past couple of weeks have contained sensational stories, with reports about men shoving women aside to get in a lifeboat and the captain “abandoning” ship. Where was the chivalry in these stories? One might argue that modern-day society has evolved away from this (I know I resisted it as a young woman, once upon a time, and that the feminist movement has encouraged equality). Pastor Tim at SimplyChurch pointed out that the blame game isn’t anything new though – that it has roots in the original sin, at the time of Adam and Eve. It’ll likely take weeks and months to sort everything out. As a previous cruise ship passenger, this hits home because ﻿I’ve always had a slight fear of a Titanic repeat when I’m on board a ship, despite it being one of the safest transportation modes (over automobile and bikes, but you hear more about plane accidents than ships too). I don’t know if I would have made it to shore or be as chivalrous, as I can’t swim and my body would likely try to save myself, hence resulting in panic, despite any intentions in trying﻿ to help others. I’m already ashamed at my selfishness.﻿ But, among these stories, we hear other tales from survivors who share the stories of heroes, those who gave up their lifejackets (and lives) for their loved ones or those who couldn’t swim, and those who stayed behind to help evacuate. But they seem to be quickly buried under the bigger headlines. Despite the tragedy, I believe it’s God’s grace that the ship didn’t sink like the Titanic, and that majority survived as this wasn’t the first time a ship sailed so close to the shoreline. Thankfully, there are people out there who put others’ lives first, including the many rescuer workers, who have spent days and nights with the search and rescue effort. Hopefully, good will result from this accident, including safer passages in the future because of stricter rules. Related links: Wikipedia entry]]>

To end 2011

gems in the city, eating (and food experiments), projects being started and incompleted (ahem), finishing my East Asian Studies certification at long last!, seeing family, and meeting new and reconnecting with friends of the past, and seeing others off (to new adventures). Many blessings, despite (dare I say?) a spiritual lack…and still trying to figure out my current (non-vocational) calling, which likely correlates. This past year, I have also noticed that my writing is full of parentheses. j.w asked me what my new year resolutions are, and I told him that I long have given up on resolutions because: a) I don’t keep them, b) I don’t like making promises I can’t keep, and c) they’re generally not that meaningful. But, plans for the upcoming year include: two weddings, hopefully trip to Ottawa and a road trip through US, finish reading some books, completing existing projects and some new (web) projects! We’ll see how it goes… I especially look forward to Christmas when it’s “my” year since we alternate years, and it’s rare that I get to see my friends and family on the other side of the country. The traditions that make up Christmas in my younger years is often the traditions that I now look forward to: a family brunch followed with exchanging gifts, and gathering with extended family. It’s time with family. And like most visits back home, it’s also friends time. Seeing friends, and often 🙂 But, my, how things have changed: what were two tall evergreens in our backyard is now the window panes and patio doors of developing condos, and the abandoned lot that once housed a bleating goat [yes, a pet goat] is the entrance of these condos! The roads are all a confusing mess, with highway exits changing, and lanes narrowing. The subway (you know you have gone away for too long when..) Skytrain has a new line that’s functional, and it’s especially strange to see a driveway full of cars that you don’t recognize that now belong to various extended family members! Our adventures?

  • Eating:
    • I certainly got my fill of sushi at 3 different places – cheap post-9PM sushi (Richmond), authentic Japanese sushi (Fuji sushi in Coquitlam), and great value Korean-Japanese sushi (Sushi Garden).
    • Drinks and dessert at Bubble Queen and Aberdeen food court (both in Richmond), though I think the place where we got 雪花冰﻿ has since declined in terms of portion size, 老婆餅 from 金都餅店﻿ (also from Richmond), coffee at Mink in White Rock, rosemary lemonade at La Fiamma, and gelato and ice cream at Bella Gelateria in Vancouver and Mallard in Bellingham. We met the owner of Bella, James, who is representing Canada and competing in the Gelato World Cup in a few days; he let us sample some of his creations and the flavors that none of us would’ve imagined would work together, truly came out well in his gelato {won’t spoil them now, maybe after the competition}
    • Soup noodles: handmade noodles at the Northern Chinese restaurant Sha-Lin in Vancouver, Japanese ramen at Santouku on Robson, pick-your-own-broth-topping-and-noodle at Cattle Cafe and Deer Garden Signatures (I like the latter more)
    • And a fill of Western food like scallops, lamb, ribeye, prime rib, and turkey at home
  • Snowshoeing: second and third time snowshoeing, this time NOT on a flat terrain. First at Cypress Mountain Provincial Park’s Hollyburn Peak, which I thought was already a challenge, but followed by Mt. Seymour’s first peak, which I found was A LOT tougher. The mild weather meant that I was warm enough in a dri-fit short sleeve shirt and a light primaloft North Face jacket, but the key is to layer as it can get windy at the top. What made the trek worth it, aside from the view, was the great grooves that you can just slide on your bum down – saves time, and it seemed safer than trying to navigate on your snowshoes down a steep incline.

Talent Attraction Lab

On the empathy map, we were to jot down thoughts that came to us around what we see, say, do, feel, hear, and think about the Region of Waterloo. The second exercise was to create a post-it map (if I remember correctly) resulting from our feelings of why we came to Waterloo, what we liked (to do) in the area, and what the area lacked (ie. what where we came from had, that the area was missing – and what was so meaningful about it). I remember posting “mountains and ocean” as something I missed, but it was more than just the scenery – it was the accessibility to the great outdoors/natural scenic trails, to spend time with people who enjoyed doing that, and access to quality seafood. The discussions that around these exercises was what was interesting: noted attitudes of the people of Waterloo versus Kitchener, the continued growth of the city (visible through infrastructure changes such as bus routes, construction, loud planes), and among all of it, I think we were in agreement that what keeps people around is their identity in the community. A community is about its people, and without them, or without people involvement, the community becomes lifeless, dull, and uninteresting; community creates the culture. This idea isn’t anything new, but I could see visually see that now, through these exercises. And, after spending over 7 years here now post-graduation, I can still be intentional about making my time in Waterloo the best it can be.]]>
