Can’t believe it’s been three years already![ref]this is in reference to Prince George but we echo their sentiments[/ref]
Though some days, it feels like more. The latest word trend after terrible twos, is not terrifying threes, but threenager. This past year was definitely a year of growth for Bean, both physically and developmentally, expressing herself more verbally, and understanding negotiation tactics, and lately, how to use a firm no. Instead of terrible or tantrum twos (thankfully), I would say teary twos, with more tears  as of late and constant[ly the same] questions, accompanied with some whining, as she flip flips from learned independence to craved dependence. She enjoys watching mommy cook and helps out wherever she can: whether it’s loading the laundry machine, putting dishes away, prepping food for cooking – I often have to remind myself that although it’s often faster to do it myself, it’s these moments that are teaching and practice opportunities, and that I will likely regret it later when she does grow up and decide helping mommy do house chores isn’t exactly fun.  She’s growing up though: almost toilet-trained (didn’t take the 3 day potty training route), figured out how to blow her nose, uses the correct personal pronouns more often, and eagerly takes on older sister roles, like feeding the baby, or watching and/or playing with him (ie. bossing him around) while mommy is momentarily occupied or keeping a watchful eye from a distance, which when he co-operates is quite wonderful as I can multi-task. She also enjoys finishing off her brother’s unfinished leftovers, which I remember I enjoyed too when my younger brother was eating baby food. Lately though, she has also reverted to not wanting to sleep on her own at night and wakes up multiple times in one night. Things that have not changed much through the first three years:
  1. She is  happy to be the cautious observer-follower instead of the leader, though she can be determined/persistent (aka stubborn, like both her parents) – which sometimes translates to being fiesty, and/or bossy. For her, being a cautious observer seems to lends well to having an eye for detail and a good memory.
  2. She hates the feeling of tears and runny nose on her face when it is caused by crying and she refuses to wipe it herself. The more you make her, the more water works result – a vicious cycle, really. Meltdowns often happen around some seemingly inconsequential thing, but it’s really a build-up of unsaid/unresolved feelings inside (maybe it’s feeling like she’s not being heard or lack of closeness / security that you’re there)
  3. Early enthused independence of feeding herself does not translate to continued self-independence

2:

In other developmental news, our almost 8-month old can now roll over from front to back, and back to front, though I think he really just rolls in one direction each way. He also can sit up unassisted for extended periods, and we have been practicing moving by trying to place items he wants a little beyond his reach. While he mouths everything, I can already sense that he is a much pickier eater than Bean was, despite his love for bananas and apples and sometimes sweet potatoes; he appears to really dislike yogurt, and maybe because it’s really cold and when upon skin contact, it turned red (like it did with peanut oil, tomatoes, and oranges for Bean). While we haven’t given rice cereal after that one time when he threw it all up, he doesn’t seem to have any problems with regular rice or barley. We need to give him more opportunities to practice grabbing food and eating it, which he has been with chunks of bananas, raw chunks of apples and celery sticks (a perfect gnawing/teething food I find), cooked zucchini and pasta tubes (rigatoni), a messy experiment as it often ends up on the floor, though I might try cooked broccoli florets or carrots next; the harder foods like apples and celery I need to watch closely as sometimes he swallows before chewing it well.  He’s still a relatively chill baby, which I am especially thankful for as I don’t think I would be able to handle two of the same temperaments; he is content to spend time in the playpen (or modern term: play yard) with his toys or chewing on a toy in his swing, preferring these two items more often than in the bouncer, and I can leave him for a short to an extended period, depending on his mood, before he complains, whereas his sister would still prefer that I am next to her all the time, if she had her way.  I’m glad our playpen is getting some use, as Bean didn’t like it back then (same story with Sophie la giraffe), although she now enjoys going inside at times to sit and play with him! He’s also been able to effectively eliminate on the mini toilet seat a few times which saved me from having to clean up a messy (cloth) diaper, but we have also encountered baby in pain while trying to strain, poor boy.

Even with help, I’m feeling drained and spent; I don’t know how single parents or those whose spouses are away do it – kudos to them. One huge advantage of the postpartum confinement practice is the help: I can get away from not having to do dishes, cook meals, change as many diapers, and entertain the toddler so I can feed the baby on demand,  get some shut-eye to make up for the little sleep I get at night. When help – in the form of either set of parents – leaves, I worry most about how to juggle the sleep schedules with two (bad parenting habits on our part as some would say, as the toddler requires too much help to fall asleep). I also hope I can figure out a way to connect with my daughter again because right now, I feel like mommy doesn’t have fun with her anymore; instead, mommy is just around to help her do things (like change diapers, feed her, bedtime routine, take her to the toilet, wash her hands) – which is probably why she’s even more resistant to other people helping her do these things these days, and would on occasion breakdown or have an outburst as a result. Lately, a typical day looks like: feed baby, change diaper, eat a meal, feed toddler, feed baby, put toddler down for a nap (short bedtime routine), power nap or laundry, feed baby, tend to toddler who has woken up within the hour, change diapers on both, eat another meal, feed toddler, feed baby, persuade the toddler to fall sleep (long bedtime routine), feed baby, some down time and snacks, and repeat the following until the morning: feed baby, nap, feed baby, change diaper, feed baby, settle toddler. (green: baby, orange: me, pink: toddler) Nights are the hardest because when I’m not sleeping, I silently will my husband to share in misery instead of serenely sleeping away or wonder why my toddler can’t easily be consoled by daddy when she wakes up in the middle of the night (and decides to cry for an hour instead).  I know these aren’t very nice (or godly) thoughts and I know it gets better eventually, but it doesn’t mean that they escape me. There has been a week of reprieve, where he actually followed a nice 2-3 hour feeding schedule, and slept nicely in between. Sadly, it didn’t last and was too good to be true.  At the time I started writing this post, he decided to feed every 1-2 hours nightly for a few days likely due to a growth spurt, but last night, it was nice to be back on a 2-3 hour sleep cycle again (funny how 2-3 hours is what to look forward these days). Though, it’s nice to have caught up with my Netflix playlist as a result. The past few weeks have gone by quickly in terms of baby’s growth: he lost 10% of his birth weight, but recovered 1 kg within a week. Perhaps it’s because the midwife wisely waited until he grew a bit before doing the heel poke for the newborn tests, but he didn’t even flinch or cry when she drew blood. While he easily tucked into my arms before, his feet now stick out when he’s feeding. He gurgles and snorts and makes all sorts of funny expressions and noises, and while he slept through all sorts of background noises the second week, it seems like he’s starting to want to be held more during the day. He’s also prone to burp and regularly spit up (even from his nose, poor guy), which I guess makes up for all the two times Bean did. This is why babies are hard work! I’m reminded why I started to enjoy being with and interacting with Bean more after she was 6 months old – by then, I probably would have a better hang of things with two as well.]]>

]]> https://emdashes.github.io/2015/08/21/first-night-away/feed/ 0 Returning Home x2 https://emdashes.github.io/2015/03/21/returning-home-x2/ https://emdashes.github.io/2015/03/21/returning-home-x2/#respond Sat, 21 Mar 2015 04:34:31 +0000 http://her.happyhippos.ca/?p=4447 ]]> Returning (to my childhood) home I hadn’t been back for an extended period for a decade so I was looking forward to spending time with friends we don’t see when we go back and seeing family more often than when we usually do when we go back (which is significant when you realize people, like your parents especially, are a decade older!). Yet, it wasn’t long before we got into a regular routine (including baby getting sick every 2 weeks and her sleeping-in) and my ambitions to bring babe to more activities resulted in only one visit to the library story time and song program, two visits to StrongStart, a visit or two to each indoor play space (Guildford Recreation Centre (1), Brentwood (1), Coquitlam Centre (2), and Lougheed Mall (1)), and one-time visits with friends and extended family we don’t normally see on our short visits. Towards the end of the trip, I realized that in my procrastination (laziness or perception that everything is so far), I didn’t end up seeing everyone we wanted – at first, the thinking was, “Oh, we have all this time…” but then by the last week or so, it became a scramble to fit everyone we wanted to see into our schedule and we had to miss some people.  But, this real-life lesson hit home for me when it comes to our spiritual state: we often tend to put God off in our lives, thinking that there’s so much time in our lives to think about that until it’s too late. What really struck me as being strange, is that I didn’t have a strong desire to go snowshoeing this time – which seems to reflect how I felt about snow activities growing up; it was not until I moved away and returned home that I wanted to snowboard and snowshoe (and ice skate). I’m not sure if it was because we acclimatized to the warmer weather that I didn’t really want to venture into the cold, whereas coming from cold makes the mountain weather more tolerable. Little Bean hit many milestones while there including walking and saying “Daddy!” for the first time, and while it took some time for her to be herself again (after being intimidated by new faces and places), I was happy to see that she found friends more her age to play with, especially at church, and that her separation anxiety seemed to taper off, which meant she was okay in the church nursery – even to the point where mommy and her friend was able to get a smoothie sans toddler in tow!

Returning (to this family’s) home

Just as it took her almost a month to adapt to her surroundings while she was away, she also seemed to feel less at ease to freely roam and explore back at home when we returned, and although the first few times back at church meant she was more clingy, she’s now back to herself and seems more open to meeting new faces and spending time with people without her parents always by her side. While I do miss the daily option to go to a free program at a local school for her, a childcare program at church, the number of free indoor play spaces, and her same-age friend and her cousin, it’s good to be back  – and we can work on finding things to do here and getting to know other children here. Upon our return, I learned a few things:
  • Buzz Lightyear somehow managed to get two hours ahead! (my side table alarm clock which seems to never be on time so I stopped resetting it every so often)
  • If you don’t want your toys and other things to be covered in dust, especially when you’re renovating, put them away or make sure they’re covered
  • And in terms of milestones, I stopped counting. Except that at 17 months, she willfully disobeyed, which when it happened, I thought hashtag #originalsin and it became another spiritual lesson: we teach our children to do many things, but we don’t need to teach them to give you a look that says they’re going to test the boundaries and then go about doing what you instructed them not to do, just as God often tells us things for our own good but we don’t listen and do the complete opposite.

    1. Gift Registries If you are considering signing up for a baby gift registry, there are a couple of options:
      • Babies “R” Us: upon placing a few items on the registry in store, a bag of freebies/samples and coupons will be given to you. If I remember correctly, a 0-6 mo pacifier was in mine, and a BPA free bottle (which we didn’t really like – we preferred the Avent bottles in the end). If you deliver on your submitted expected date (or your guess), they will give you a gift as well.

    2. Work Benefits
      • Employee Assistant Programs: Through my employer’s employee assistance program, we got 3 books (A What to Expect book, a year one book for Daddy, and a board book), a Baby Einstein lullaby CD, a mini Whoozit toy, and some other generic literature
      • Health insurance and fitness credits: this covered a private ward for recovery after delivery, and also allowed me to buy a jogging stroller as well as a breast pump
    3. Brands Even though you may plan to breastfeed exclusively, you might need formula as emergency supply or even to give away to other expectant/new moms since formula actually costs a fair bit!
      • Nestle Baby: you need to select that you plan to formula feed to get the backpack with change pad I’ve been told, some formula, and maybe a bottle?
      • Enfamil: formula
      • Johnson & Johnson used to give out a free travel pack of baby products if you request it from their contact form, but when I did, they sent me a voucher to claim one in store (which I haven’t found a place that carries them yet), and when my sister inquired, she was just directed to healthyessentials.ca to request non-baby related samples. You can now sign up for an account at healthyessentials.ca, fill out your profile to say when you’re expecting and they will automatically give you a form to fill out so that they can send you the the voucher.
    4. Stores
      • Shoppers Drug Mart: VIB – free pack of diapers (I actually received two, but it could be because the fine print said offer valid until Dec 31, 2012 so I emailed them about it; my sister only got a coupon for diapers, I think), a baby bib, and coupons for 1000 Optimum points for buying various products, like baby Tylenol, diapers, drinks and snacks. Be sure to opt in on Optimum emails as they seem to send diaper coupons every so often (my sister has gotten $8 off any size pack of diapers and some other freebie). Update: they discontinued the program sometime near the end of 2016.
      • Sobeys Baby Be Healthy: free monthly vitamins up to a year (you just need to go once a month), reuseable bag, a couple of Size 2 Compliments brand diapers – these perks may only be given to you if you sign up in store (at the pharmacy) Update: I was able to sign up online and get a small gift bag in store, and this time it contained a package of Huggies Little Snugglers newborn diapers!
