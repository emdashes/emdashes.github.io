Thankfully my sister’s kids are the same size, so I was able to limit my luggage to just a carry-on suitcase, a backpack (with snacks and activities), one car seat, and a diaper bag. I didn’t have to take a car seat with me, but I decided that having one on board would be safer and better overall, so I had to find a way to lug it without needing to carry it.

The suitcase stroller

I looked online for ideas, and saw a couple of products to help mitigate this problem – one was a foldable trolley where you put the car seat (and child in) and the other was a strap that looked like a T where it attached the car seat to a carry-on suitcase. Well, I didn’t want to buy anything that was only useful in this situation or bring along anything extra (like the trolley), so I tried to use what I had to mimic the T-strap. A mom in a local mom’s Facebook group asked me to post some screens of my setup when she saw a photo of what I did, and j.w wanted to record a video of it, so he did a wonderful job of capturing my explanation, Bean and all:

For those who don’t want to watch the unedited video, read on for some written instructions.

Materials


This is what I used – and what you might need:

  • An adjustable luggage strap. You might be able to get away without this if the LATCH straps on your car seat are long enough to wrap around to the back of the suitcase; mine cannot. It seems that Asian families back in the day were known to wrap their suitcases with luggage straps (to keep it together and to see it easier on the carousel, maybe?), so it was quite easy for me to find one lying around. I like this particular one because there is a loop in it that the carabiner clips in so it’s snug and it doesn’t move. I’ve also used a bungee cord in a pinch, when I left my luggage strap behind – but I didn’t feel it was safe to put a child in it then.
  • One carabiner. If it’s weight supported (such as ones meant for climbing), it’s probably safer; mine’s not.
  • A key ring, O-ring, or D-ring. You might be able to get away without this if the top tether can clip to the carabiner, but I find it much easier with it. Probably safer if it’s weight-tested as well. If you get rid of your luggage strap, you would need this so that the LATCH clips can clip/hook onto the ring.

The setup


A three-step summary:

  1. Match the back of the car seat against the front of the carry-on suitcase when vertically standing
  2. Thread the luggage strap through the car seat where the seat belt would go for a forward-facing set up, and adjust so that car seat is snug against the suitcase. If there are handles on your suitcase, put the luggage strap through them (the video shows the straps are twisted – try to make sure they’re flat before continuing).
  3. Take the top tether of the car seat and thread it through the handles on top of the suitcase and the handle bar (I pull up the handlebar to make it easier), and hook and clip the tether to the ring, or the carabiner, if you don’t use a ring. Tighten, tilt, and test it out.

With the car seat strapped to the carry-on suitcase, I could either put all my stuff into the seat while Bean helped roll it, or she sat in it and helped hold some stuff, while I wore my 18 month old in a carrier. People looked our way, but I’m not sure if it’s because people haven’t seen others do this, and/or they were thinking what kind of crazy parent would do that. On Air Canada (Dreamliner specifically), I can roll this contraption down the aisle and get to my seat without undoing anything, but it does not fit down the aisle on WestJet or Air Canada Rouge planes – some will fit down business class but once you get to economy, you will quickly find out it doesn’t fit so it’s better just to undo it in the area where parents gate check their strollers and car seats. You will also need to buffer a little more time to take it apart and put it together again as you go through security screening.
Happy travelling!

And yet, each bottle became recycled waste every time it emptied because one soap pump on sale was cheaper than a bottle of soap refill.
Then, I became a parent.
I read about how triclosan, an ingredient in these antibacterial soaps aren’t the best choice for little hands, because they kill everything along with other questionable things that are associated with it. It seems like they’ve banned this ingredient now, at least in US, which is a good start, and that Bath & Body Works has “gentle foaming hand soap” now in its place. And, I started to shift towards more natural products, preferring unscented or scented with essential oils over added (synthetic) fragrance or parfums.
Eventually, I quit buying from Bath & Body Works and saved the last few bottles once they were empties and have used my own foaming soap since, which is probably a more frugal and more environmentally-friendly alternative. [ref] Glass or stainless steel dispensers is probably best, but I’ve used plastic without any problems so far (I did toss one when something looked off).[/ref] Interested in making your own?

Easy as 1-2-3:

You can follow the many recipes on Google but here’s my version:

  1. Get some liquid castile soap (like Dr. Bronner’s) – the just-under 1L bottles of castile concentrate go on sale sometimes for under $15. If you want, you can buy unscented or scented and skip adding fragrance in later. [ref] just be careful that you don’t get any of it undiluted in your eye or your face, especially the peppermint one, as it is super concentrated and your eyes will burn and your skin will tingle for a long while – trust me on this one, I know from experience[/ref]
  2. Add some soap into an empty foaming dispenser
  3. Add water – I do 1/4 or less of soap and the rest with water. You can experiment with a ratio you like by washing your hands and feeling how clean it is after.

Pro tip: I don’t actually fill up the entire bottle; I rather make the foaming soap solution more frequently to lower chances of having any bacterial growth / residue in a bottle that sits unused. Note that if it sits in a cool area, it may cloud over, which is fine.

Optional:

  • Use distilled water instead of tap, as I’ve read that it may keep bacteria and floaties (precipitate as my friend calls it) at bay. I’ve just used filtered and haven’t run into any trouble since I switched from tap.
  • For your own custom blend or to add a nice scent, add only a few drops of essential oils (do you research and avoid the ones that may have a risk if you have little ones in your household: rosemary, peppermint, eucalyptus are at the top of my mind to avoid); sweet orange (be careful as acid may react with plastic in the bottles; I haven’t had that problem) and lavender may be good choices to start.
  • Add a few drops of Vitamin E for moisturizing and to possibly help with extending its shelf life, but Dr. Bronner’s has tocopherol as an ingredient which some labels will also say Vitamin E as a preservative.
On the featured image: Since I don’t have time to make my bottles fancy with custom labels to show off, perhaps a DIY faucet extender hack will go well with DIY foaming soap?

Stakeholder: one who has a stake in the enterprise, and involved in or affected by a course of action, who is going to be impacted by what you’re putting out. The majority¬†of people¬†will not be the perfect stakeholder.
Always remember: it’s a two-way relationship.

Steps

  1. Know your actor(s)

    • their¬†knowledge of design, their belief in design, and

      their level of influence – keeping in mind that driving change or adoption from top-down is much easier than from bottom-up
    • could fall into one of four quadrants / types:
      1. prospect – low belief, low in understanding of design
      2. skeptic – low belief, high in understanding; may become an obstacle or distraction, being a disruption in meetings, and might even rub off on others and lower the energy
      3. eager beaver – ¬†low in understanding but high in belief
      4. champion – high in understanding and in belief in design
    • understand their background or need
    • building empathy with stakeholders is vital
    • Recommended reading: Design Thinking in Harvard Business Review

  2. Write the script

    • language is important: adopt the language of the people / of the organization
    • Find out what the words mean; define your context
    • understand your stakeholders; understand their mental models:

      e.g. the word “research” could have different meanings for¬†many people (“market research”, “academically”, qualitative vs quantitative);
      there is a risk of miscommunication when one person has a different mental model than another;
      physical mental models (e.g. a house) are easier to illustrate than service experience mental models (e.g. getting your morning coffee/tea)
    • Group icebreaker idea: sketch the steps on how each of them would make a cake; how much detail will one insert into a sketch? this helps you to gain insight on what they value and surface assumptions – where people are at, what they are thinking
    • Reminder: you might get¬†buy-in into the process, not necessarily into the product / idea, and that could still be considered a “win”

  3. Set the stage

    • adapt activities to¬†who is in the room,
    • workshops:¬†to get people on the same page
      types:

      1. showing:¬†for skeptics, prospects; high-production in quality, simple
      2. learning:¬†for prospect, eager beavers; e.g. Design 101; case-studies, templates, examples to work through together, coaching opportunities such as journey maps, empathy maps
      3. working:¬†for champions, eager beavers; have clear objectives, advanced tools, and less prep work is usually necessary; the dream state

]]> https://emdashes.github.io/2017/03/13/blnd2017/feed/ 0 Web: Moving from Desktop to Mobile https://emdashes.github.io/2017/03/10/web-moving-from-desktop-to-mobile/ https://emdashes.github.io/2017/03/10/web-moving-from-desktop-to-mobile/#respond Fri, 10 Mar 2017 18:00:42 +0000 http://her.happyhippos.ca/?p=5603 Taken from the archives of my UX blog, started some years ago. Originally posted: October 22, 2010¬†| Revisited today, as we’re using¬†mobile more than ever before.
uxWaterloo is a local group that meets regularly with folks interested in the user experience of products and services. This month, James Wu, a UI Architect, spoke at the meeting about design considerations for mobile experiences.¬†This event particularly interested me, not only because of the topic, but particularly also because the guest speaker is from Kobo, a Canadian¬†(at the time) e-book company (that integrates with Pocket, so you can download and read everything you’ve saved online offline and without the ads!).
Like many, I have traditionally focused more on web desktop experiences, but I have on occasion needed to design for a mobile experience as well. James eloquently summed up the challenges I have often come across when transitioning to the mobile space, and reminded me of others that aren’t always thought of, such as:

Screen Real Estate

  • Screen size (diagonal space)
    For example, a 29″ desktop monitor could be 300-500% bigger than a mobile phone
  • Orientation – the changes from portrait and landscape (and vice versa)
    What’s interesting is that during their user research with 1024×600 tablets (such as the Samsung tablet), they observed that users prefered using this size of tablets in landscape format
  • Screen resolution
    Using the same 1024×600 example, you would either have your screen in “short and wide” format, or “narrow and long”
  • On screen keyboard
    On many touch devices, more than 1/2 the screen is taken up by the keyboard, which would cover anything underneath it
  • Platform variations¬†(or¬†device / hardware variations)
    The takeaway? Design for different experiences/products; don’t cram everything into one screen if it’s not necessary. Why? “Don’t necessarily think people use their phones the same way as tablets [desktops, etc.].” James said that at Kobo, they must design knowing that people will have multiple devices; the experience should be consistent across them, but not [necessarily] identical.

Hardware

Like desktop computers, hardware varies across different models. For the mobile devices, things like processor and memory are important to consider as it could affect battery life, performance, and even result in side effects like heat. Others include:

  • Removable storage: the ability to “hot swap” a media card while the application is running in either the background or the foreground could cause undesired effects if designed improperly
  • Interaction mechanisms: these may effect how a user interacts with an application such as trackballs, trackpads, keyboards, gesture strips (like the Palm Pre)

Gestures
For touch devices, we get into the a realm that is still pretty new. There is not yet official standards for touch gestures and there is limited documentation to illustrate them (James suggested that Apple may currently have the leading edge).

  • Core/standard gestures like tap, swipe, pinch, expand, drag
    For example, a more common gesture is the Pinch-Zoom to adjust the font size of Apple devices
  • Peripheral gestures may include less familiar gestures such as long press, rotate, shake, sequences
    Using Apple again for this example, a less common might be shaking the iPhone to perform the undo action

When designing for touch, one must consider gestures could be used in different directions and even with multiple fingers. It isn’t surprising that some devices even recognize simultaneous touches (imagine designing a piano keyboard app so that multiple keys could be played at once to create a chord).
It’s also important to provide users with affordances that let them know that features are there, and that they will use. Kobo uses instructional overlays for first-time use, as well as ghosted elements. I tried to imagine what a “ghosted element” was based on James’ description and thought of the many mobile¬†games¬†where they use these visual cues¬†to indicate the controls (where to tap on the screen) as a part of the onboarding experience.
Kobo may also add advanced gestures for more advanced users (like a keyboard shortcut on the desktop), such as adding an element of fun by adding a gestural easter egg in the Kobo app.

User Context

We must remember that users are likely in different environments and may have more constraints than if they’re sitting in front of a desktop. Considerations include:

  • Mobility: device may not be plugged in (may impact battery life for example)
  • Divided attention: users could be multi-tasking or looking at the device on the go
    [e.g. using their devices while walking from the train platform to catch the connecting train]
  • Unpredictable surroundings: things like flakey connections [e.g. online then loses reception while train goes under the tunnel] could impact a download or chat experience
  • Cost: How much would it cost by running your application? Unfortunately, not everyone has unlimited data. Some customers pay per byte, and that might mean customizing for wifi vs. data
  • Others: the amount of light (dark environment) or even the number of free hands could impact how they interact with the device.

I’ve observed commuters carrying a briefcase in one hand, newspapers in another, and managed to hold a cup a coffee, along with their device. In that case, could they still perform a multi-touch gesture, and would they still be able to navigate even if they couldn’t?
Knowing what users are doing while they’re using your product is a good advantage. Lesson from Kobo: James told us of a time when they hid the notification bar, including the time, to minimize distractions while the user was reading. Yet, it didn’t occur to them that if a customer didn’t have a watch or an easy way to check for time, they had to exit out of the application. Now, the notification bar can be brought back with a gesture.

Standards and Expectations

Customers might expect applications to be specific to their product. When Kobo designed an app that looked like it was for Apple device, Android customers weren’t happy. But once they took away the “Apple” button and changed the color, they noticed a decrease in complaints about the look of their application.
So when designing for a mobile experience…
…these are just some considerations and potential challenges that may come up while designing for a mobile product. Others are shared across the desktop too. We can’t forget who we’re designing for, the primary user for your product. For Kobo [at the time], it’s for women in the mid 30-40s, simply because they found that they spent the most money. Who knows, you might even need to take accessibility concerns as a requirement, simply because it’s the law.

Starter Brew

Fully brewed kombucha should smell like vinegar (or a faint smell if it’s not too strong), and a formula for starter brew comprises of: 1-2 cups of full brewed kombucha (the starter liquid) + SCOBY that is at least 1/8 inch thick (5oz) and should cover at least 1/2 of the surface area / opening of the jar/container/vessel, which is added to¬†1 gallon brewed tea; the more SCOBY you have, the more likely you will succeed. What’s a SCOBY? It stands for Symbiotic Culture of Bacteria and Yeast.

  • A healthy one can look like a thin puck and¬†it’s not unusual to see spits, holes, strands, floaties and strings of yeast[ref]edible, but most people filter it out[/ref].   • Making an ideal brew: it thrives in 73-85F temperature, out of direct sunlight and avoid an exterior wall if possible.
