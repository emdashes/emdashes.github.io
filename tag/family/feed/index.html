I’m one month in. And yet, it feels like a lot longer than that. I’ve found a rhythm that works with this new routine, and I’m thankful that both our kids have adjusted well too. Consequently, we transitioned our oldest to full day kindergarten, and after some trial and error, we dropped her afternoon nap, we eat dinner earlier, and they go to sleep earlier. Unfortunately, our youngest still wakes up at night and asks for food then, but our oldest seems to fall asleep faster and stays asleep throughout the night for the most part. This week has been one of the few germ-free weeks this year, so it’s a nice relief to not have to deal with that (if it wasn’t him, it was her, and if it wasn’t either of them, it was us or our daycare) – I’m grateful that our workplaces are pretty flexible when it comes from work from home policies and taking time off / emergency days, so we pretty took turns staying home with one child or both children. How do working families who don’t have backup plans / caregivers manage?
At the beginning, it sort of felt like I was starting at a new school in the middle of the school year; I had a lot to learn and a lot of faces and names to remember. I still have a lot to learn and faces and names to remember, but it feels like I’m making an impact. I’m also thankful that I enjoy my work, so it’s easy to wake up earlier and rush out the door.
We will have to find a different routine come summer, but so far, it’s been working out just perfectly. I’m glad I scaled back my hours (before signing the offer) because it turns out if you’re driving a car for school pick-up, you pretty much have to arrive in the neighbourhood at least 20 minutes early in order to find available street parking around the perimeter of the school; otherwise, you’re pretty much out of luck. And even though I get off at 2:30PM, by the time I get everyone home, it’s 4PM. I then give them a small snack, work on unloading the dishwasher and getting another load in, and starting supper. By the time we are done dinner, it’s close to 7PM, and we play a bit then bathe (if applicable), and then off to bed. After that, I clean up the kitchen (pack food away, prep lunches) and maybe wash or fold the laundry. I’ve been doing laundry on weekends in loads, so I can just sit and binge-watch Netflix (working through The Crown) while folding.
Surprisingly, I find myself dozing off before midnight and using the computer less after work (but still on my phone) – maybe it’s because I no longer have mid-day naps or work is more tiring than staying at home (though I feel it’s a break / nice change from home life). I’m on a modified work week, and I’m already wondering how full-time working parents do this every day and survive? I applaud those families!
Other than work and school, we started going to a different small group / life group, which has changed to an earlier start time, which means for the first time in a long time, we actually get to stay past dinner. We have also started going to another church, primarily because Bean is at an age where she is learning, and I simply can’t go on teaching her every week – and when I don’t, I hear implied comments about going to a different church anyway. I’m a little sad, but since I also miss fellowship time because I’m watching them after service is over while everyone else is chatting and because people don’t tend to eat together for lunch as much anymore, I’m not really connecting with anyone either. I know Bean misses it because on occasion, she tells me she wants to go back to Simply, or that she wants me to be her teacher that day, and every time we pass by St. Paul’s, she points out that it’s our church. Finding a new church to call home is hard. Personally, I don’t like the format of the sermons by the lead pastor at this new church so far (grossly paraphrased: partial verses are taken from different translations to fit into the sermon’s points) though the supporting pastor actually goes into a passage in contrast, but the kids seem to have adjusted so well even at the first visit and a lot of effort is put into the children’s spiritual wellbeing and education. So yes, 2018 is going to be a year of change and adjustment.

Two years ago, we were celebrating two month milestones. Now, at 2, he’s come a long way:

  • Speaks in complete sentences. Has started to communicate (and copy) in English. Boo.
  • Enjoys climbing and doing crazy things and will deliberately test boundaries with people in authority (me – he’s heavy so I can’t just pick him up and grab him while grocery shopping for example, so I don’t go when I’m on my own as it’s impossible to get more than one or two things in one trip)
  • Loves to sing, and can sing many songs in English including the Alphabet song, Twinkle Twinkle Little Star, Away in the Manger, Roly Poly and can sing along with or recognize songs by Walk Off the Earth, Moana, and Frozen.
  • Loves trains, especially Thomas. And cars. And airplanes. Prefers trains though, if given a choice.
  • Easily sucked in by technology: photos on the phone, TV, iPad game, and YouTube videos. One time early on, the caregiver gave an older child a tablet to use while the younger ones were supposed to nap; he had a meltdown and refused to sleep. Then she figured out why (we had warned her prior so she made the connection and no more problems since).
  • Has not completely weaned. So, I’m still off dairy and some nuts. Nuts!
  • While he started off with liking meat, he doesn’t seem to like meat (fish the exception) as much anymore, unless it’s chicken nuggets/fingers/crispy. He will still eat it but when it is in chunks, he tends to pick it out. He also doesn’t like green fiber veggies, but will repeatedly down it in smoothie form. Like his sister, he loves fruit. Avocados, bananas, and cereal with alternative milk are now his favourite breakfast foods. He is a soup and rice monster. Sometimes noodles too.
  • Self-toilet training is hit and miss so far – and if successful, it’s still only #2 (though a few times, he wakes up with a dry diaper and will go if you put him on the toilet); progress seems to have slowed down or regressed especially now that he doesn’t tell his caregiver he needs to go (or maybe she hasn’t learned the signs yet)
  • He may have had a mini febrile seizure the other week, when he had a slight fever (while fighting off strange rashes – maybe mild symptoms of hand, foot, mouth + reaction to lactose in Tylenol) but it lasted no more than a few seconds and it was in his sleep.
  • He seems interested in people he knows (and babies) – often willing to share a toy, asking for someone by name, offering hugs to those he loves (except it’s sad to see hugs not reciprocated, especially when his sister comes out from dance class and he goes to hug her and she brushes him off; however, she always asks to go into timeout with him when he’s in trouble), and enjoys helping out in the kitchen (putting dishes away, watching and helping mommy cook/bake)
  • He must have shorter legs or something, because he fits his age in tops when it comes to sizing but his bottoms are behind a bit.

I’m amazed at how fast our children is growing up, now that we all have slightly different routines and that they are no longer babies.

]]> https://emdashes.github.io/2018/02/13/the-start-of-something-new/feed/ 0 Back to school https://emdashes.github.io/2017/09/04/back-to-school/ https://emdashes.github.io/2017/09/04/back-to-school/#respond Mon, 04 Sep 2017 04:36:05 +0000 http://her.happyhippos.ca/?p=5800 It’s the time of the year again: it’s back to school season, and with only a few days remaining of summer, I’m starting to feel a bit anxious about school – and I’m not even the one that’s starting school!

Homeschooling

I have been reflecting a bit about school for the past few months – in fact, this post started as a post about homeschooling back in May, as more and more families we knew were homeschooling or planning to homeschool. I briefly considered it, and even paid to check out the KWCHEA‘s homeschool conference exhibit hall to look at resources and get a better sense of what is available for a homeschooling family.  While buying into curriculum and paying for supplementary classes to supplement a child’s education / learning can be costly (vs. free public school education), I found that there were plenty of free resources (and you could develop your own curriculum as a result) and quite a bit of local support (e.g. free gym time for homeschooled children, email lists) so that you could still have social interaction with other peers. I also found that there were a few memberships that were free to join if your oldest child is under 6 years old,  which gives you access to a support network and perks like (significant) discounts to places like Great Wolf Lodge. But, what a vendor said got to me – partly because my first feeling was “no, it’s not!” but then I know that there is some truth to what he said: he basically told me that home education was God’s calling for all Christian families because the responsibility lies with the parents to teach their children and because that was how it was done historically/biblically (ie. why leave it to other people to do that). I thought about the examples in the Bible, and how children learned their father’s trade as an apprentice (yes, that supports what he’s saying), but then didn’t the young men go off to the synagogue to learn scripture from the pharisees / teacher of the law? And if it’s God’s calling for every Christian family to homeschool their children, that must mean that most are ignoring His calling – is that possible and true? With homeschooling, I know I don’t have to be as worried about the impacts of early sex education, or the in-your-face celebration of gender rights and gay pride, but I don’t want to shelter my kids either.
At first, I didn’t want to consider homeschooling my children because my first exposure to a homeschooler wasn’t a very positive experience, when a student who had attended a public elementary school, then moved to being homeschooled (maybe due to her being teased for being a Jehovah’s Witness?), and then in high school tried to transition back, but she didn’t last very long and was pulled out. So, I’m biased to think that the transition from home education to public might be really difficult and not successful.

Public education

Among the selfish reasons why I didn’t want to homeschool is that I didn’t want to deal with the social isolation, or forcing myself and the children to interact with other homeschooled families – or trying to find ways to be socially involved, be it signing up for supplementary classes ($) or leading co-op-like initiatives. Then again, while I excelled and really enjoyed the academic parts of school, I don’t think I really felt like I truly fit in socially (oh, the insecure me, but perhaps it’s reflective of what the Bible says about being in this world, but not of it, or being foreigners[ref]”If you were of the world, the world would love you as its own; but because you are not of the world, but I chose you out of the world, therefore the world hates you.” Matthew 5:19 ESV[/ref]). Maybe it was due to a combination of language struggles, cultural differences, and a Christian upbringing; I’m not really sure.[ref]Case in point, 6-year-old-me awkwardly learned that just because the teacher is Chinese, it doesn’t mean that a) she speaks Chinese and b) that she can read my name in Chinese, when the worksheet says “Name:” on it. Likewise, just because she is Chinese, it also doesn’t mean she knows what 白花油 is and its medicinal purposes – and will tell you to take it off because it smells -_- (embarrassed of my mom moments) . Though, I was super happy to find a Chinese student (Canadian-Chinese) who appreciated my baked BBQ pork bun lunches.[/ref]
One downside of following the crowd, kids these days are entering the education system as early as 3 years old, and at full days – without naps – too! Great for paying parents as they can save on childcare expenses, but for those young ones who have meltdowns because of missed naps, personally I think it’s too much, too soon (probably because my generation started as young as 4 years old only at half days). Actually, it’s nice that kindergarten is optional for families here, but if I hold them back, I feel that they would miss out on key social interaction development and other learning milestones and will lag far behind when starting Grade 1, especially if one throws in French immersion into the mix.
I pray that her social experiences be better than mine, especially as she navigates the first few years of school! And I pray as a parent, that we’ll be responsible for keeping on top of what is being taught so that we can supplement with truth and a biblical worldview when necessary.

Change

As we transition into a new chapter of our lives (including new morning routines and making school-friendly lunches), I’m comforted that even though I was a bit disappointed to learn that there are 6 kindergarten classes in her school and none of her friends (or the other couple of kids we know) are in the same class, she is really excited and looking forward to starting school (much more so than when I teach her in Sunday School). After going through the Get Ready for School program, she has loved programs and have done so well in them.  When people ask me if we’re ready, this blogger mom sums up my feelings quite well: “She was so ready – that made one of us.  I’m not sure if I’m finally relieved to have some time to myself [or more time with the youngest, rather] or if I’m just terribly sad”.  I know little brother will miss her dearly too, and I’m sure I won’t know what to do with just him at the beginning but here’s to change, God willing!

But here I am, back from a trip to Vancouver and lived to tell that not only did we all survive, no crying tears were shed on the plane – and those of you who know our littlest, you probably remember that when he is left by himself for any period especially in an unfamiliar environment with unfamiliar faces, he starts to wail in no time, but with God, all things are possible!

Last minute trip

A few weeks ago, j.w found out that his company was sending him to Vegas. The initial plan was that we leave Bean with the grandparents for a few days while taking the baby with us. Except Vegas was 45C, feeling like 50C outside, and it isn’t exactly the most baby-friendly place. Plus, I’ve been to Vegas before and decided that being there once was enough.  So that left me with the option of staying home with the kids, in Toronto with the kids, or…I could pay an extra ticket and take all the kids with me to visit family in Vancouver; after all, Bean has been asking about when she could visit her cousin for months now – plus, I could help with kid-sitting since my mom’s sprained ankle wasn’t much better so she couldn’t take my sister’s kids. Turns out, my brother-in-law’s brother (does that make him brother-in-law too?) – who has no kids – was really awesome with the kids; they all loved him, and by the end of it, my crying boy loved him and Bean even chose him to drop us off at the airport –  over her aunt and grandma!  I could even leave him with 4 kids while I did dishes – made me think that maybe that’s what having an au pair feels like. We both concluded that two adults watching four kids is so much easier than one adult watching two, especially with the girls and boys being so close in age – the girls often went off giggling and up to no good, while the boys were okay in each others’ company, even though they didn’t really play together much – other than around the train set. We also talked about how having more children at a home daycare is better because of this and you get paid more. Sleep was probably the biggest challenge, with him waking up at 5-6am the first few mornings, and having them sleep in on the two days that count: for church and the day of departure. Thankfully, with the two girls sleeping in one room, I didn’t really have to be with her although I felt bad that I often neglected her to try to put the youngest to sleep. My admiration for single parents have increased tenfold, especially if they don’t have any other family support around them to deal with sleep time, meals, meltdowns, and accidents. Trips out west generally feel really short even if it is longer than two weeks, but it seemed that 10 days was good enough this time – despite missed connections with old friends (we tried!); by the end, I felt I needed another vacation after this one.

On the plane

Speaking of departure, there’s nothing like hearing your name on the intercom at the airport along after the generic “final boarding call for flight xxxx  to Abbotsford; paging passengers L and W” that gets your legs going – while towing a preschooler in the car seat that’s strapped around the carry-on suitcase [ref]thanks to an idea from the internet – I saved $30 and used a non-weight tested luggage strap, carabiner and keychain ring, and the car seat tether instead – probably would be safer if I had used a weight-tested carabiner and O-ring instead [/ref] and carrying the baby in the ergo – and feeling sweaty by the time you settle into your seat on the plane. Thankfully, we were at the end of the second moving sidewalk already when I heard my name so it wasn’t too far for me to sprint there and wave, “we’re here – wait for me!”.
I don’t know what happened to the time, but we left church late, met with some traffic on the road, and of course, everything just takes longer with kids; thankfully, I had no luggage to check-in (another great thing about having cousins close in age and size). But by the time we were through the family line in security, I looked at one of the screens and noticed that there was only 15 mins left until departure so instead of taking the friendly security officer’s advice to wait for a golf cart (I did go there to see if a cart was around). Actually, everyone was super kind at security and on my flight – they let me take the baby through in the ergo and didn’t even scan it, and perhaps I was last on board, but they offered to take my (heavy 25lb+) suitcase and ended up stowing it overhead up front (I told them I didn’t need anything in it, so I would also be okay if they decided to check it) and carrying my car seat into our row, while I took care of the kids and the backpack. As we were settling into our row, one of the flight attendants strongly suggested to the passenger in our row to move to another row – back 10 rows! She politely declined, saying that she was fine, but with some prompting from the flight attendant saying that it would give us some wiggle room and that she wouldn’t be sitting beside anyone, she finally agreed, and I thanked her with relief. Had I known that there were empty rows, I would’ve volunteered to go back, but I had asked if it was a full flight, and the answer I got was that there were only a few seats here and there. So we got a whole row to ourselves, with was great, as it allowed the kids to spread out and take turns in the car seat and the other seat.


Unfortunately, Bean didn’t sleep at all (thus missing her nap) and little brother only got in a 30 minute nap at most both on the flight there and back. I do celebrate mini-successes though: on the first flight, I  did manage to transfer him into the car seat so that I could take her to the washroom. The second time she had to go, I picked a child-friendly show I had previously downloaded from Netflix on the iPad and gave him some snacks to munch on; alas, when I had to go the third time, he didn’t like it, so I took him with me. On the way back, he didn’t complain at all! Snacks (apple chips, puffed rice crackers, and baby food pouches, and the plane’s apple juice diluted with water) and new toys / activities (stickers, coloring, mini puzzles) kept them sane, but what worked on the 5 hr flight there didn’t work for the flight back – I only needed to take out 3 activities (1 for the boy) and the snacks for the short 3.5 hr flight back (wow, that’s some tail wind!), perhaps because the Dreamliner had the in-flight entertainment console (minus the sound – didn’t bother with earbuds), and the really cool shade button that adjusts the brightness and darkness of the passenger window pane. While we only got two seats to share among the three of us, it seemed more spacious than the Air Canada Rouge plane to Abbotsford – roomy enough for the boy to nap on my lap (again, for less than 30 minutes because he woke up upon transfer when Bean needed to use the lavatories) and for him to stand and sit on the floor in front of my seat.

I am hoping it will still be warm enough to try water beads with them and sidewalk chalk fun then, and maybe even the mini parachute from Amazon Prime Day.

While I only have to endure it until the end of our breastfeeding time together, our little one might have to watch his diet for the rest of his life – I’m sad for him, but at least he won’t have memories of enjoying those, so he may have less cravings for them, and hopefully better self-control (than I do!)[ref]I didn’t realize how much I regularly enjoy milk products until now, even though I don’t care for milk itself[/ref]. 
I once suspected that milk might have been the culprit in his bouts of eczema and bloody stools[ref]though the latter might still be as a result of constipation[/ref], when he was known to spit up after every feeding (I would say mild reflux), and experienced three vomiting episodes after eating one serving of rice cereal that contained milk ingredients, but I was in denial; how could it be? My daily diet only consisted of milk in my one cup of espresso – or so I thought; I forgot about the cheese, ice cream, butter, everything else that has milk protein in it. I’m pretty sure I was in denial, so I didn’t cut anything in my diet, thinking that it wasn’t really an allergy, and like his sister, would outgrow it. Now, after a visit to an allergist and a positive prick/rash/allergy test, it’s official: he has a milk and peanut allergy.
So now, our low-maintenance baby has become a high-maintenance one; I guess our “easy” baby was too good to be true. Yet, this news comes at a timely moment in our lives; we’re reading through The Power of a Praying Parent by Stormie Omartian (I keep thinking it’s “O’Martian”), and we just covered a chapter that talks about releasing our children to God, and this is an example of a very much an appropriate moment to follow her model of prayer: that if it’s in His will and in His time, that our son will outgrow both his allergies; but if not, will be managed well so that no one would ever need to use an epipen on him; but if so, that it is administered successfully and effectively.
I’m still processing the news, so I didn’t get a chance to think of and ask the many follow-up questions that have since popped up (stemming from online research and what others have shared with me – yep, I’m also one of those internet moms), but here are some questions that people have asked and the answers I have come across:
Q. Will he outgrow his allergy?
A. Possibly. Our allergist says that many children outgrow their milk allergy by the age of 5. I just found out that my siblings and I all had milk allergies until we were 1 years old, and our mother experimented by trying to reintroduce milk slowly and over time. However, only 20% of kids outgrow their peanut allergy. Because Bean seemed to have a milder reaction to peanut butter when she was younger, but outgrew it by 2 years old – which surprised him, the allergist suggested paying for a blood test to see if her brother would qualify for reintroduction of peanuts under clinical supervision; we’re praying that he’d be eligible. So, as of now, it is what it is – will just have to adapt to manage it.
Q. How severe is his allergy?
A. At the skin prick test, the one for peanuts was about 5mm, and the one for milk was slightly larger (6mm, maybe?); I think the size indicates a positive diagnosis, but I didn’t think to ask. According to one site, “no test […] can determine severity“. Nevertheless, we’ve been advised to keep two epipens with him. While, I don’t know the criteria for what makes an allergy a risk for anaphylaxis, we have seen symptoms matching the criteria in Table 3, section 2 (GI and skin symptoms) and in this table.
The allergist prescribed two injectors (as does this site), but I think most people I know only carry one. Although he does not meet the minimum recommended weight of 15 KG (33 LBs), the allergist still recommended that over a self-administered antidote citing that human / user error is very common. He then used an ultrasound machine to measure the thickness of muscle between the skin and the bone, to make sure that our son’s thighs is fat enough – more than 13mm – so that the injection won’t hit bone (and bone marrow, which is equivalent to a IV administration and that’s bad); he was 16mm. Another option is available (TwinJet?) but is supposedly the same dose.
Q. Is there no other option other than Epipen? What about Benadryl?
A. The allergist and online sources tells me that Benadryl won’t help with breathing, but it may help reduce other symptoms (e.g. puffy eyes, lips).
Q. What can he drink instead of milk, to get the fat content and other nutrients that is in whole milk?
A. My dietician and online sources say that breastmilk is best so to keep breastfeeding for as long as possible. As long as we have the go ahead to eat soy, we can also have soy and coconut milk; there’s rice and almond milk as well. I’ve tried hemp milk, and it tastes like grassy straw, which I’m not a fan of at all. Fortified alternatives provide at least 30% calcium and some fat content, but it doesn’t completely replace milk’s benefits; it’s also a shame that many companies use carrageenan (or xanthan gum, but not as bad) to thicken their drink, and add sea salt to flavour it. I have to remember to continue to supplement him with Vitamin D. For good fat, I will likely have to be more creative, using oils like avocado or olive, and serving more salmon (frozen filet steaks of wild Alaskan salmon is readily available from Kirkland / Costco year-round, works out to be 10$/lb which is reasonable)
Q. What about formula?
A. Online sources seem to suggest that if a baby has a milk allergy, baby may have a higher likelihood of developing a soy allergy, so switching to a soy based formula may end up increasing the risk.
Q. Can he have lactose-free, cheese, or yogurt? What about goat’s or sheep’s milk?
A. Unfortunately, the allergy is to the milk protein, not the lactose. Most people with milk allergy is to cow’s milk protein, but I’m not sure if we have the go-ahead to try other kinds of dairy.
I did mention that I had accidentally fed him vegetables cooked in butter a couple of times and failed to see a reaction, to which he was surprised, so as a result, he told me keep to doing it, but don’t go beyond what I normally would have done. We will likely have to monitor him very, very closely if we do that – not to mention, we’ll have to watch him even more closely when we aren’t the ones providing his meal / food. Though, I wonder if he can tolerate extreme heated milk products – will have to ask.
Q. Can he have other nuts?
A. In theory, yes. Peanuts is technically a legume and not a tree nut (e.g walnut, pecan), but we have to be careful of cross contamination because they may be processed in facility that isn’t peanut free.
Q. What about peanut oil?
A. I’m not sure about this one. Online sources say that pure unrefined peanut oil should be safe, but I need to check with the allergist about this one. Will likely have to avoid though, especially in commercial places, like restaurants – because can you be sure that it’s pure and unrefined?
Q. So, are you a peanut and dairy-free household now?
A. Thankfully, complete elimination measures aren’t necessary at this time. In fact, the allergist told us specifically not to stop giving our daughter peanut butter! However, Mom will also need to follow a similar elimination diet, which will be a challenge, as she can’t eat anything from the allium family either (e.g garlic, onions, chives, leeks).
Q. What can I do to help?
A. Pray for us – I (we) have no idea what I’m (we’re) doing. Trying to avoid garlic, onions, etc. is already hard; I’m also thinking that avoiding dairy will likely be more challenging than peanuts. And, if you eat with us, we especially appreciate that you take our dietary needs into consideration – understandably, it’s probably especially hard if you don’t need to conscientious of it in your everyday life; I’m sorry for the inconvenience. If you’re in a similar family situation, I’m open to hearing advice and your experiences too. Thank you!

Here’s what I learned:

  • The (now world-wide) blue box recycling program started in Kitchener
  • The waste management site “hires” two hawks to send seagulls away a few times a week
  • Mount Trashmore, our local popular tobogganing hill, was discontinued as a landfill in the ’70s
  • Landfills that are at max capacity are capped with ceramic before being topped with soil and grass

  • A tarp-like sheet goes over every layer of garbage to keep it from blowing away and fumes and moisture are sent through pipes, where the methane gas is collected and converted into energy; I didn’t see any burning pipes anywhere, nor did it smell nearly as bad as the one we used to drive back next to Highway 1 back home before it got converted to a driving range
