Around 6:30 AM one morning, Bean woke up and didn’t go back to sleep but was content to lie in bed with me. At some point, she told me that something was bothering her at the back of her head (her one-worded summary: “ow”), near the nape of her neck. In my sleepy haze, I parted her hair and took a peek. A skin-coloured lump, almost the size of my pinky fingernail, puzzled and troubled me so I tried to get a closer look with a tissue because I didn’t want to touch it because it looked like it had hair follicles, which also sort of resembled short insect-like legs. So, trying to hide my “heebies jeebies”[ref]the “heebie jeebies” accurately sums how I feel, as used by one forum poster with his experience[/ref], I asked her if she wanted Daddy or Grandma to come in to help me, and she said Grandma, so I went to get a second opinion. Grandma later told me she took a video and some photos and sent it to a doctor friend of hers, who admitted he didn’t know what it was, perhaps an insect bite or an infection with a sac. We made an appointment with our family doctor, and I started googling and looking for images of skin tags that matched what I had seen – nothing that looked that size, even though the description seemed to match.
At the doctor’s office, the nurse and the doctor took a look and confirmed that it was a skin tag. Her explanation was in line with what I had read online: that they’re benign, often made of the same cells and components of your skin, so you will see things like hair follicles, and that this one had a stalk. I couldn’t figure out how I missed it (since I comb her hair daily), but I asked how long it could have been there for, and she explained that it could have been there for awhile but was hard to spot because it wasn’t bothering Bean. She told us that it look like it was going to auto-amputate because the stalk end/base was starting to turn bluish, so we had a few options:

  1. Leave it be, and see how long it would take. Usually for smaller skin tags, it takes 2-3 days, but this one could be slower because of its size.
  2. Use liquid nitrogen to burn it off. I asked how the pain would be compared to a needle, and she said it would probably be the same intensity but longer.
  3. Something similar to #2 but with local freezing. We opted to go with this.


For whatever reason, she decided to take a second look, and this time, IT MOVED. I appreciate that she didn’t freak out, because I would have – and certainly did inside. Instead, she calmly said that she was going outside to get a second doctor’s opinion and help. The second doctor was a lot more certain that it was a tick and they partnered to quickly remove it with a pair of tweezers; it was still very much alive in the urine sample jar they put it in, so we assume that every bit came out (thus reducing Lyme disease, if at risk) – the second doctor must have really wanted to make sure for he must have pulled extra skin and hair off, because Bean started crying even more and I was handed a piece of gauze to apply pressure to stop the bleeding[ref]a week later, the spot looks raised with a dark scab, and it must feel tender because it still bothers her[/ref]. Google helped identify the tick as a dog tick, which isn’t as great of a threat as the black-legged ticks, but they jarred it anyway to be sent for analysis. She called a contact to see if we needed to start antibiotic treatment, as their guidelines only offered advice to those over 8 years of age. Later, she also followed up to tell us that Public Health would call if they found something of concern and texted us some close-up photos. We don’t have a dog, but we did go to the urban natural area the day before, and that’s likely where we picked it up.
I also appreciated that she apologized and retracted her first diagnosis; she had seen ticks before, but none this size[ref]I don’t blame her – Google tells me that it was an adult female in its partially fed state so it would be bigger[/ref]. She’s a family doctor, so I can’t expect her to know everything, even though we hope they know what ails us. She recognized that even though I had made comments about characteristics reminding me of an insect, it was likely that everyone’s quick glance assumptions as a skin tag threw everyone off.

I like being outdoors. I enjoy aspects of camping, canoeing, and especially hiking; I remember my dad would take us on walks and bike rides around trails when we were much younger. In all my years doing activities outdoors, I would have never imagined having to deal with this. Thankfully, I think this experience was more traumatic for me than it was for Bean, since she didn’t see what was bothering her, despite some tears and pain.
Thank God that Bean noticed it, and He answered our prayers, giving wisdom to our doctor who for reason decided to do a second  look – because who knows if we would’ve found it otherwise. Plus, I am really glad that I didn’t have to remove it myself.

Language

In under two playdates with a 4-year old, her English vocabulary and frequency of use has exploded; now, it’s almost as if her English switch gets turned on as soon as she steps into her play area. Whether she use words in the right context, or put together nonsensical sentences or words which I know is her attempt at English, she isn’t afraid to try – and persist when we don’t understand her. When she first started speaking (signing and Cantonese), we understood her – it was almost as if she saved up all she learned before speaking, so this babbling is new to us! I’m certain she understands more than she can express, which generally happens when one learns a new language; it’s easier to pick up a language when reading and listening than speaking and writing.
As I listen to her speak more and more English day after day (which I worry she might soon forget her Chinese), I wonder how much more quickly we would pick up a language if we adults approached language learning that way: not being afraid to just go for it even when you might not make sense.
For some of her Cantonese, sometimes I can tell who she has picked it up from based on the words she uses. For example, if I hear “啜筒” (zyut tung), I know it’s from Joseph and his side of the family, because I say “飲筒” (yum tung); likewise with “裡頭” (lui tou), I say “裏邊/面” (lui bian/mian) instead.

Music

She still enjoys singing songs and will spontaneously break out in song all day long, especially ones with actions – either ones that she’s been singing at Music and Movement, the library, or ones I’ve been teaching her – so I am trying o find Christian songs that are easy to learn in Cantonese to pass on (so far, I’ve managed Jesus Loves Me with 2 missing words, and parts of two other songs I sang in my childhood).

Colours and Letters


She’s also at the point where she will make and remember associations, so I’ve found success with helping her identify colours and alphabet letters, although sometimes, she will just say  the association, like “Daddy” instead of “D” – but I know that she recognizes that letter now, because she says the same association every time. Other letters she recognizes is O, “Breanna” or “Baby” B, “mama” E (maybe because of my name?), “doggie-bone” I, sometimes “moon-crescent” C and “snake” S,  and consistently “Leia P” (but it’s really an A). This opens up new activities to try, such as at restaurants, instead of doing word search on the kids menu, we do letter search.
For colours, we’ve progressed from doggie brown to sunshine yellow and fire engine red – and she just somehow knows the colour green, so it’s her favourite colour of the moment. She can sometimes also identify cloud/blueberry blue, purple and pink. With her new-found love for green, she has also begun to collect objects of the same colour and sort them.

Doing


She has started to assert more independence (which often leads to doing especially when told not to do), or wanting to do more things on her own, so I’ve asked her to help me get a diaper and wipe when changing her brother, pick out her table setting and bring it to the table at meal times, (re)wash her bib after she’s done with it, and let her figure out how to put on a pair of elastic-waist pants and bite my tongue when it ends up being backwards.

I know many children at this age can put on clothes, socks, jackets, etc. but we haven’t mastered those skills yet. Toilet-training is also slow progress – some days she knows and will tell me she needs to go, and other days, she is happy to pee in her training underwear and not care or seem to notice the couch has gotten wet (still thankful she self-trained for poop for over a year now).

Meals are still spent half self-feeding, with help from an adult once she gets bored with self-feeding (or isn’t as hungry anymore), especially with food choices that aren’t as easy to self feed because it requires utensils or may be messier, like soup noodles or rice with soong; she can pretty much eat an entire sandwich or a serving of sushi rolls on her own though.

When it comes to art and drawing, she’s starting to be able to trace around objects and the other day, she coloured within the lines – we have yet to find out if that was an accident or not. She is starting to cut by following lines with scissors and thread objects through a pipe cleaner, but she’s not at the point where she does it repeatedly / enjoys it yet. 
This afternoon’s blog post is brought to you by the peace and quiet of two children napping away, a first! Will report back at three; hope it won’t be the terrifying threes.
