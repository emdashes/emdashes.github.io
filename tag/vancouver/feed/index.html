Last minute trip

A few weeks ago, j.w found out that his company was sending him to Vegas. The initial plan was that we leave Bean with the grandparents for a few days while taking the baby with us. Except Vegas was 45C, feeling like 50C outside, and it isn’t exactly the most baby-friendly place. Plus, I’ve been to Vegas before and decided that being there once was enough.  So that left me with the option of staying home with the kids, in Toronto with the kids, or…I could pay an extra ticket and take all the kids with me to visit family in Vancouver; after all, Bean has been asking about when she could visit her cousin for months now – plus, I could help with kid-sitting since my mom’s sprained ankle wasn’t much better so she couldn’t take my sister’s kids. Turns out, my brother-in-law’s brother (does that make him brother-in-law too?) – who has no kids – was really awesome with the kids; they all loved him, and by the end of it, my crying boy loved him and Bean even chose him to drop us off at the airport –  over her aunt and grandma!  I could even leave him with 4 kids while I did dishes – made me think that maybe that’s what having an au pair feels like. We both concluded that two adults watching four kids is so much easier than one adult watching two, especially with the girls and boys being so close in age – the girls often went off giggling and up to no good, while the boys were okay in each others’ company, even though they didn’t really play together much – other than around the train set. We also talked about how having more children at a home daycare is better because of this and you get paid more. Sleep was probably the biggest challenge, with him waking up at 5-6am the first few mornings, and having them sleep in on the two days that count: for church and the day of departure. Thankfully, with the two girls sleeping in one room, I didn’t really have to be with her although I felt bad that I often neglected her to try to put the youngest to sleep. My admiration for single parents have increased tenfold, especially if they don’t have any other family support around them to deal with sleep time, meals, meltdowns, and accidents. Trips out west generally feel really short even if it is longer than two weeks, but it seemed that 10 days was good enough this time – despite missed connections with old friends (we tried!); by the end, I felt I needed another vacation after this one.

On the plane

Speaking of departure, there’s nothing like hearing your name on the intercom at the airport along after the generic “final boarding call for flight xxxx  to Abbotsford; paging passengers L and W” that gets your legs going – while towing a preschooler in the car seat that’s strapped around the carry-on suitcase [ref]thanks to an idea from the internet – I saved $30 and used a non-weight tested luggage strap, carabiner and keychain ring, and the car seat tether instead – probably would be safer if I had used a weight-tested carabiner and O-ring instead [/ref] and carrying the baby in the ergo – and feeling sweaty by the time you settle into your seat on the plane. Thankfully, we were at the end of the second moving sidewalk already when I heard my name so it wasn’t too far for me to sprint there and wave, “we’re here – wait for me!”. I don’t know what happened to the time, but we left church late, met with some traffic on the road, and of course, everything just takes longer with kids; thankfully, I had no luggage to check-in (another great thing about having cousins close in age and size). But by the time we were through the family line in security, I looked at one of the screens and noticed that there was only 15 mins left until departure so instead of taking the friendly security officer’s advice to wait for a golf cart (I did go there to see if a cart was around). Actually, everyone was super kind at security and on my flight – they let me take the baby through in the ergo and didn’t even scan it, and perhaps I was last on board, but they offered to take my (heavy 25lb+) suitcase and ended up stowing it overhead up front (I told them I didn’t need anything in it, so I would also be okay if they decided to check it) and carrying my car seat into our row, while I took care of the kids and the backpack. As we were settling into our row, one of the flight attendants strongly suggested to the passenger in our row to move to another row – back 10 rows! She politely declined, saying that she was fine, but with some prompting from the flight attendant saying that it would give us some wiggle room and that she wouldn’t be sitting beside anyone, she finally agreed, and I thanked her with relief. Had I known that there were empty rows, I would’ve volunteered to go back, but I had asked if it was a full flight, and the answer I got was that there were only a few seats here and there. So we got a whole row to ourselves, with was great, as it allowed the kids to spread out and take turns in the car seat and the other seat. [caption id="attachment_5855" align="alignright" width="225"] Activities for the plane – most of it was under $3, sourced from places like Walmart, Dollar Tree, Wish, and Chapters.[/caption] Unfortunately, Bean didn’t sleep at all (thus missing her nap) and little brother only got in a 30 minute nap at most both on the flight there and back. I do celebrate mini-successes though: on the first flight, I  did manage to transfer him into the car seat so that I could take her to the washroom. The second time she had to go, I picked a child-friendly show I had previously downloaded from Netflix on the iPad and gave him some snacks to munch on; alas, when I had to go the third time, he didn’t like it, so I took him with me. On the way back, he didn’t complain at all! Snacks (apple chips, puffed rice crackers, and baby food pouches, and the plane’s apple juice diluted with water) and new toys / activities (stickers, coloring, mini puzzles) kept them sane, but what worked on the 5 hr flight there didn’t work for the flight back – I only needed to take out 3 activities (1 for the boy) and the snacks for the short 3.5 hr flight back (wow, that’s some tail wind!), perhaps because the Dreamliner had the in-flight entertainment console (minus the sound – didn’t bother with earbuds), and the really cool shade button that adjusts the brightness and darkness of the passenger window pane. While we only got two seats to share among the three of us, it seemed more spacious than the Air Canada Rouge plane to Abbotsford – roomy enough for the boy to nap on my lap (again, for less than 30 minutes because he woke up upon transfer when Bean needed to use the lavatories) and for him to stand and sit on the floor in front of my seat. [caption id="" align="alignleft" width="240"] Ikea was selling there bento-like lunch boxes for half price (@$2) so I picked up a couple which ended up being great for the plane ride back. Highlights

This trip felt very different because it was more about spending time with my sister’s family than seeing friends, trying new places, or going out. We did go out – often fitting the kids in 4 car seats in my sister’s Rondo + 2 adults in front, but it was to do kid-friendly activities, which were:
  • [caption id="" align="alignright" width="240"] Folks don’t seem to bat an eye when kids take the things on the showroom floor and use them for a bit…[/caption] A visit to Ikea for play and lunch: this happens regularly in my sister’s family, but Bean was finally tall enough to play in the ball room so it was her first time; we took the boys to the unsupervised kids area in the showroom to play. At lunch, one of the staff pointed out the computer that was near the cash that lists all the ingredients in their food – the fish and chips were not safe (contains dairy), but the chicken fingers were safe so I got a kids meal instead with fries.
  • $1 swim at the local community centre: on Tuesdays and Thursdays, it’s only $1 a person, and the warm pool and communal change room is very large (with private changing stalls and showers in the latter) and you have a lot of accessories to use like life-jackets, floating boards, toys, and duck boats, plus you only need a quarter to lock up your stuff in the lockers AND you get your money back when you open it again. Don’t bring a water camera into the pool though, they don’t allow it. The littlest in our group ended up falling asleep on a duck boat when my sister took the two girls to shower.
  • A meal at Red Robin meant you had unlimited sides – and they make the birthday girl stand up in her (high) chair when they sing their birthday song. I like independent restaurants but I have discovered that chain restaurants, like Red Robin, tend to be much better at providing ingredients / allergen information for each menu item. By the way, if you have the app, if you end up earning the 10th burger free on your visit, it will automatically trump any other promotion you may be offered, including 20% off your bill. Each kid also gets to take a balloon home where it is played with for days after.
  • [caption id="" align="alignright" width="320"] Looking out one of the many windows on the Seabus[/caption] A day trip to ride the bus, Skytrain and Seabus: this in particular brings up memories of my aunt taking me to do this as a child; we didn’t use public transportation as a means to go somewhere, we just used it for the ride. The kids seemed to enjoy it as much as I did way back when. This time, I got to see and use the (finally done) Compass system for the first time, and going on a Saturday meant that everything was one-zone fare – and we managed to make all our connections before the time was up each way! There’s a small but free twirly slide into a pit of balls at Lonsdale Quay, but I forgot socks for my kids, so I took them to see the coin rides instead. I didn’t have money, but the $1 double decker London bus ride would’ve been the best value as it could’ve easily fit 4 kids in it – plus there’s a TV screen inside that acts like a window. Splurged on some local peaches and coconama chocolate because they had some nut and dairy-free versions (I’ve sampled some before but I can’t remember where – perhaps at the Handmade Show at the PNE). We were treated to some live music by a very talented young boy while we had our lunch, and I was surprised that my son ate an entire dynamite roll for lunch.
  • [caption id="" align="alignright" width="180"] Kaleidoscope play in the early years area[/caption] A visit to Science World: they have added a relatively enclosed section [ref]with a staff member at the entrance monitoring who goes in/comes out – hoping to keep stray kids inside I think[/ref] dedicated for 0-5 year olds, which was amazing; they could have easily spent their entire day in this gallery – it had a climbing structure (no slide, but I’d recommend bringing socks), sensory play areas like water features, giant lite brite panel, kaleidoscope, balls, foam building blocks and plastic pipes, and a scoop-and-dump oatmeal-like area. The girls didn’t care for most of the shows, but we all enjoyed the bubble show on the main stage. The best part is that my sister found a 2-for-1 admission promotion posted on their site for those that can read Chinese. Odd, but we used it!
  • [caption id="" align="alignright" width="234"] Blueberries by the bunches[/caption] Blueberry picking: I’ve picked strawberries, raspberries, and blackberries but I don’t recall picking blueberries before – they just fall off the tree in clusters when you give it a tug! Thankfully the kids didn’t really notice the long detour and traffic until closer to the end of our drive.
  • Rocky Point: unlimited chips with kids fish and chips meal at Pajo’s, my chance to try the Tacofino food truck at last (the fish burrito though big and great in value, was slightly disappointing as the fish didn’t stand out – should have done two fish tacos instead)  – happy to find out that their batter is vegan, and sorbet from Rocky Point ice creamery (though their ice cream is really the way to go) while the kids had a splash at the splash pad and played on the playground for a bit before heading home. Bought some locally made beef jerky at the pop-up market as it’s the first time I’ve seen jerky without any garlic or onions!
On the other days, the kids were happy to play in the yard with bubble guns and on the make-shift slide that their uncle made; the water table made its first appearance outside, and was actually filled with water (that I had forgotten was gifted to Bean when she was born – but was then re-gifted because there was no way would we lug a table back). So thankful that the weather was really good during the time we were there – not too wet and neither too hot nor too cold!  Now, I’m looking forward to when my sister and her two come to us in September – she decided to brave the flights too! I am hoping it will still be warm enough to try water beads with them and sidewalk chalk fun then, and maybe even the mini parachute from Amazon Prime Day.]]>

]]> https://emdashes.github.io/2016/12/08/4-weeks-recap/feed/ 0 Returning Home x2 https://emdashes.github.io/2015/03/21/returning-home-x2/ https://emdashes.github.io/2015/03/21/returning-home-x2/#respond Sat, 21 Mar 2015 04:34:31 +0000 http://her.happyhippos.ca/?p=4447 ]]> Returning (to my childhood) home I hadn’t been back for an extended period for a decade so I was looking forward to spending time with friends we don’t see when we go back and seeing family more often than when we usually do when we go back (which is significant when you realize people, like your parents especially, are a decade older!). Yet, it wasn’t long before we got into a regular routine (including baby getting sick every 2 weeks and her sleeping-in) and my ambitions to bring babe to more activities resulted in only one visit to the library story time and song program, two visits to StrongStart, a visit or two to each indoor play space (Guildford Recreation Centre (1), Brentwood (1), Coquitlam Centre (2), and Lougheed Mall (1)), and one-time visits with friends and extended family we don’t normally see on our short visits. Towards the end of the trip, I realized that in my procrastination (laziness or perception that everything is so far), I didn’t end up seeing everyone we wanted – at first, the thinking was, “Oh, we have all this time…” but then by the last week or so, it became a scramble to fit everyone we wanted to see into our schedule and we had to miss some people.  But, this real-life lesson hit home for me when it comes to our spiritual state: we often tend to put God off in our lives, thinking that there’s so much time in our lives to think about that until it’s too late. What really struck me as being strange, is that I didn’t have a strong desire to go snowshoeing this time – which seems to reflect how I felt about snow activities growing up; it was not until I moved away and returned home that I wanted to snowboard and snowshoe (and ice skate). I’m not sure if it was because we acclimatized to the warmer weather that I didn’t really want to venture into the cold, whereas coming from cold makes the mountain weather more tolerable. Little Bean hit many milestones while there including walking and saying “Daddy!” for the first time, and while it took some time for her to be herself again (after being intimidated by new faces and places), I was happy to see that she found friends more her age to play with, especially at church, and that her separation anxiety seemed to taper off, which meant she was okay in the church nursery – even to the point where mommy and her friend was able to get a smoothie sans toddler in tow!

Returning (to this family’s) home

Just as it took her almost a month to adapt to her surroundings while she was away, she also seemed to feel less at ease to freely roam and explore back at home when we returned, and although the first few times back at church meant she was more clingy, she’s now back to herself and seems more open to meeting new faces and spending time with people without her parents always by her side. While I do miss the daily option to go to a free program at a local school for her, a childcare program at church, the number of free indoor play spaces, and her same-age friend and her cousin, it’s good to be back  – and we can work on finding things to do here and getting to know other children here. Upon our return, I learned a few things:
  • Buzz Lightyear somehow managed to get two hours ahead! (my side table alarm clock which seems to never be on time so I stopped resetting it every so often)
  • If you don’t want your toys and other things to be covered in dust, especially when you’re renovating, put them away or make sure they’re covered
  • And in terms of milestones, I stopped counting. Except that at 17 months, she willfully disobeyed, which when it happened, I thought hashtag #originalsin and it became another spiritual lesson: we teach our children to do many things, but we don’t need to teach them to give you a look that says they’re going to test the boundaries and then go about doing what you instructed them not to do, just as God often tells us things for our own good but we don’t listen and do the complete opposite.

    ]]>     https://emdashes.github.io/2014/12/05/age-and-nostalgia/feed/ 0     First family vacation https://emdashes.github.io/2014/07/22/first-family-vacation/ https://emdashes.github.io/2014/07/22/first-family-vacation/#comments Tue, 22 Jul 2014 10:00:05 +0000 http://her.happyhippos.ca/?p=3994 ]]> Celebrity Millennium [caption id="attachment_4027" align="alignnone" width="640"] Celebrity Millennium at Icy Strait Point on a rainy day[/caption] Initially, I had wanted to cruise with Holland America, after hearing so much good reviews with them, but we ended up settling with Celebrity Cruises instead. Even after we booked, our travel agent managed to find a better deal: Little Bean cruised for free, plus we got a stateroom upgrade for the same price! Seeing that we would be limited to our cabin when Little Bean was sleeping, we paid a premium for a balcony so that we could sit outside and read or appreciate the view despite the chilly weather – though of course, after staying in a balcony stateroom from our honeymoon, we haven’t been able to downgrade again. This line was also an upgrade to the other cruise lines we have traveled with in the past, and despite my initial reservations about them being owned by Royal Caribbean (which we had negative experiences with on our previous cruise), we were very happy with our experience overall. Aside from the glitches at the beginning with a non-functional stateroom fridge and missing baby food (yes, you can pre-order baby food jars beforehand at no extra charge!), this one seemed less in-your-face cheesy, the decor less tacky, people were more orderly (less chaos on the “shopping nights”), and the food quality and execution to be better (the pastries and desserts at the café were a highlight, especially the almond croissants). Service seemed less forced, but wasn’t absent either; dining room staff went above and beyond to switch us from set time dining (and shared table) to our own table flexible dining times, and our server brought Little Bean her own plate of food that wasn’t even on the menu (avocado, watermelon, and other melon cubes) even though we always had a jar of baby food with us. We were pleasantly surprised that we got a complimentary tour of the kitchen galley, unlike other cruise lines, and learned just how vast the kitchens are and how much is made from scratch (including the lovely pastries and desserts)! I also appreciated that the activities they offered on board went beyond activities like basic ballroom or napkin folding, like basic watercolours, and that you could borrow toys from the children’s area for the duration of the cruise. Furthermore, if your wee one spent any time in the children’s area, playing with any of the toys, they took care to disinfect it at the end of the day (provided the parent tossed it into the used-toys bin). Okay, so the targeted demographics on this cruise line is definitely not your Disney or Royal Caribbean Oasis/Allure of the Seas ships; there are no rock-climbing walls, water slides, zip-lines, carousels, costumed characters, or cinematic theatre under the stars (the latter mayhaps because of the chilly weather, seeing that the outdoor pool was also closed). But, the performances, though not Broadway, were solid; j.w was impressed that the lead performers could both sing and dance at the same time. With the limited options for evening activities, that meant that we could retire early without feeling that I missed too much. I was certainly surprised to learn that this cruise was the first cruise for many of the older folks we met! Little Bean made new friends quickly: Maria, the host at the dining room, the Metropolitan; Azem, the assistant Maitre’D (who just adores babies, and even introduced her to his fiancée over from the Q’Sine to meet her on the last night, which I thought was the sweetest thing); and little Emma, daughter of a God-fearing  father who serves in the Air Force, and whose Christian mother shared about her struggles with transitioning to a stay-at-home-mom.

    The Weather

    [caption id="" align="alignnone" width="640"] A rainy day at Icy Strait Point[/caption] Although we had hoped for sunshine and warm weather (which they had a week before our sailing as we heard from one of j.w’s former pastors that he randomly bumped into at the Anchorage airport),  the clouds and rain followed us for the most part, but it didn’t deter us from seeing the sights; in fact, I think because of it, we met less people along the way. God kept us dry for the most part though and gave us sunshine in patches, which meant staying cool and having more dramatic photos; the day it poured, we were either watching whales from our balcony, or driving along the Skagway highway (we had a different view on the way back, when it cleared up!). The $8 stroller cover also kept our little one dry. Plus, we had almost a week of sunshine and warmth in Vancouver! The inclement weather may have been the reason for the rocky waters during the first two days from Seward to Juneau; I just ended up sleeping early when I started to feel it in the claustrophobic washroom in our stateroom. Turned out that many others fared worse – the mom we made friends with had to skip the rest of her dinner, and one of the shows had to be postponed to another day. So, what did we see in Alaska?

    Alaska – Glaciers

    When we first chose Alaska, I had doubts with the destination choice as my mother didn’t seem to like the itinerary when they went a decade ago; she had found the ports relatively uninteresting. However, she did say her favourite part was visiting Hubbard Glacier, so we made sure that was included in our itinerary. Ironically, I was actually disappointed with it the most, perhaps because I was expecting a grandeur that my mother had described. Sadly, perhaps it is because of the environmental impact and decay over the last decade or because of the poor weather, but we didn’t get as close to the glacier as expected and it looked much smaller and less majestic than the photos that my parents had of it when we compared photos later. Fortunately, we managed to see a few other glaciers as well, including Trail Glacier as seen from the train trip from Anchorage to Seward, Exit Glacier in Seward, which you can almost touch when you walk right up to it, and Mendenhall Glacier in Juneau. [caption id="attachment_4045" align="alignnone" width="640"] Mendelhall Glacier, Juneau[/caption]

    Alaska – Wildlife

    Oddly enough, one of my favourite ports was Icy Strait Point, a fishing village that is close to Hoonah that’s really only dedicated to cruise ship traffic. The sights to see around the port didn’t seem all that terrific, especially on a rainy day, but we were rewarded with views of whales along the shoreline and from our balcony. We returned to the ship much earlier than the sailing time, but we had great views of pods of fin whales despite the rain. Apparently on the port side, those in the buffet dining room and on a kayak excursion had a treat; they saw with 6 whales playing together, whereas we, on the starboard side, saw 3 at a time. We also saw many bald eagles flying about throughout our trip, notably in Icy Strait Point and in Juneau (the capital city of Alaska, where no roads lead out of Juneau; you can only arrive via air or sea). Most of the other wildlife we encountered was during our 5 hour train ride from Anchorage to Seward: a family of grizzlies, a black bear, mountain sheep, and young caribou dashing across the pond. The one other “wildlife” we came across was a family of slugs in Ketchikan, which I found amusing that people were grossed out over them on Facebook, but perhaps I grew up seeing slugs everywhere that it merely served as a reminder of the West Coast climate.
