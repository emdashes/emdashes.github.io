We just need to find childcare now, but if God has provided all of this, I’m certain we will be in good hands!

]]> https://emdashes.github.io/2017/04/08/the-day-we-had-our-first-ambulance-ride/feed/ 2 We are one of those families… https://emdashes.github.io/2017/01/25/we-are-one-of-those-families/ https://emdashes.github.io/2017/01/25/we-are-one-of-those-families/#comments Wed, 25 Jan 2017 17:00:56 +0000 http://her.happyhippos.ca/?p=5543 Today marks the end of steamed milk in my coffee, grilled cheese sandwiches, peanut butter pretzels and buttered popcorn, yogurt, ice cream, and other products containing milk, possibly soy, and peanuts in my diet until the end of our breastfeeding days, which might be for another year or more. While I only have to endure it until the end of our breastfeeding time together, our little one might have to watch his diet for the rest of his life – I’m sad for him, but at least he won’t have memories of enjoying those, so he may have less cravings for them, and hopefully better self-control (than I do!)[ref]I didn’t realize how much I regularly enjoy milk products until now, even though I don’t care for milk itself[/ref]. 
I once suspected that milk might have been the culprit in his bouts of eczema and bloody stools[ref]though the latter might still be as a result of constipation[/ref], when he was known to spit up after every feeding (I would say mild reflux), and experienced three vomiting episodes after eating one serving of rice cereal that contained milk ingredients, but I was in denial; how could it be? My daily diet only consisted of milk in my one cup of espresso – or so I thought; I forgot about the cheese, ice cream, butter, everything else that has milk protein in it. I’m pretty sure I was in denial, so I didn’t cut anything in my diet, thinking that it wasn’t really an allergy, and like his sister, would outgrow it. Now, after a visit to an allergist and a positive prick/rash/allergy test, it’s official: he has a milk and peanut allergy.
So now, our low-maintenance baby has become a high-maintenance one; I guess our “easy” baby was too good to be true. Yet, this news comes at a timely moment in our lives; we’re reading through The Power of a Praying Parent by Stormie Omartian (I keep thinking it’s “O’Martian”), and we just covered a chapter that talks about releasing our children to God, and this is an example of a very much an appropriate moment to follow her model of prayer: that if it’s in His will and in His time, that our son will outgrow both his allergies; but if not, will be managed well so that no one would ever need to use an epipen on him; but if so, that it is administered successfully and effectively.
I’m still processing the news, so I didn’t get a chance to think of and ask the many follow-up questions that have since popped up (stemming from online research and what others have shared with me – yep, I’m also one of those internet moms), but here are some questions that people have asked and the answers I have come across:
Q. Will he outgrow his allergy?
A. Possibly. Our allergist says that many children outgrow their milk allergy by the age of 5. I just found out that my siblings and I all had milk allergies until we were 1 years old, and our mother experimented by trying to reintroduce milk slowly and over time. However, only 20% of kids outgrow their peanut allergy. Because Bean seemed to have a milder reaction to peanut butter when she was younger, but outgrew it by 2 years old – which surprised him, the allergist suggested paying for a blood test to see if her brother would qualify for reintroduction of peanuts under clinical supervision; we’re praying that he’d be eligible. So, as of now, it is what it is – will just have to adapt to manage it.
Q. How severe is his allergy?
A. At the skin prick test, the one for peanuts was about 5mm, and the one for milk was slightly larger (6mm, maybe?); I think the size indicates a positive diagnosis, but I didn’t think to ask. According to one site, “no test […] can determine severity“. Nevertheless, we’ve been advised to keep two epipens with him. While, I don’t know the criteria for what makes an allergy a risk for anaphylaxis, we have seen symptoms matching the criteria in Table 3, section 2 (GI and skin symptoms) and in this table.
The allergist prescribed two injectors (as does this site), but I think most people I know only carry one. Although he does not meet the minimum recommended weight of 15 KG (33 LBs), the allergist still recommended that over a self-administered antidote citing that human / user error is very common. He then used an ultrasound machine to measure the thickness of muscle between the skin and the bone, to make sure that our son’s thighs is fat enough – more than 13mm – so that the injection won’t hit bone (and bone marrow, which is equivalent to a IV administration and that’s bad); he was 16mm. Another option is available (TwinJet?) but is supposedly the same dose.
Q. Is there no other option other than Epipen? What about Benadryl?
A. The allergist and online sources tells me that Benadryl won’t help with breathing, but it may help reduce other symptoms (e.g. puffy eyes, lips).
Q. What can he drink instead of milk, to get the fat content and other nutrients that is in whole milk?
A. My dietician and online sources say that breastmilk is best so to keep breastfeeding for as long as possible. As long as we have the go ahead to eat soy, we can also have soy and coconut milk; there’s rice and almond milk as well. I’ve tried hemp milk, and it tastes like grassy straw, which I’m not a fan of at all. Fortified alternatives provide at least 30% calcium and some fat content, but it doesn’t completely replace milk’s benefits; it’s also a shame that many companies use carrageenan (or xanthan gum, but not as bad) to thicken their drink, and add sea salt to flavour it. I have to remember to continue to supplement him with Vitamin D. For good fat, I will likely have to be more creative, using oils like avocado or olive, and serving more salmon (frozen filet steaks of wild Alaskan salmon is readily available from Kirkland / Costco year-round, works out to be 10$/lb which is reasonable)
Q. What about formula?
A. Online sources seem to suggest that if a baby has a milk allergy, baby may have a higher likelihood of developing a soy allergy, so switching to a soy based formula may end up increasing the risk.
Q. Can he have lactose-free, cheese, or yogurt? What about goat’s or sheep’s milk?
A. Unfortunately, the allergy is to the milk protein, not the lactose. Most people with milk allergy is to cow’s milk protein, but I’m not sure if we have the go-ahead to try other kinds of dairy.
I did mention that I had accidentally fed him vegetables cooked in butter a couple of times and failed to see a reaction, to which he was surprised, so as a result, he told me keep to doing it, but don’t go beyond what I normally would have done. We will likely have to monitor him very, very closely if we do that – not to mention, we’ll have to watch him even more closely when we aren’t the ones providing his meal / food. Though, I wonder if he can tolerate extreme heated milk products – will have to ask.
Q. Can he have other nuts?
A. In theory, yes. Peanuts is technically a legume and not a tree nut (e.g walnut, pecan), but we have to be careful of cross contamination because they may be processed in facility that isn’t peanut free.
Q. What about peanut oil?
A. I’m not sure about this one. Online sources say that pure unrefined peanut oil should be safe, but I need to check with the allergist about this one. Will likely have to avoid though, especially in commercial places, like restaurants – because can you be sure that it’s pure and unrefined?
Q. So, are you a peanut and dairy-free household now?
A. Thankfully, complete elimination measures aren’t necessary at this time. In fact, the allergist told us specifically not to stop giving our daughter peanut butter! However, Mom will also need to follow a similar elimination diet, which will be a challenge, as she can’t eat anything from the allium family either (e.g garlic, onions, chives, leeks).
Q. What can I do to help?
A. Pray for us – I (we) have no idea what I’m (we’re) doing. Trying to avoid garlic, onions, etc. is already hard; I’m also thinking that avoiding dairy will likely be more challenging than peanuts. And, if you eat with us, we especially appreciate that you take our dietary needs into consideration – understandably, it’s probably especially hard if you don’t need to conscientious of it in your everyday life; I’m sorry for the inconvenience. If you’re in a similar family situation, I’m open to hearing advice and your experiences too. Thank you!

I didn’t even know these were considered pocket diapers until much later. However, I’ve moved away from stocking velcro-based diapers, because they stick to each other and they seem to wear out faster.
Maybe I am not as meticulous when it comes to taking care of our stuff, but now they are well-used and well-loved in comparison to looking like-new when we first received them (they have been through one child prior). Because the elastics in the legs are also getting more and more relaxed, I’ve designated these to our toddler and ended buying more diapers to add to my collection, but they’re not brand-name diapers and they’re all snap-based instead of velcro. They seem harder to put on and take off, but they don’t seem to wear out as quickly either. I have also added bamboo diapers and inserts into my collection, and while they seem more soft and absorbent than my microfleece diapers and inserts, stains seem harder to wash out from natural fibers than synthetics. Because we’re using the bamboo diapers with our baby, I’m new to EBF stains that turn blue and green after a wash/dry cycle – can’t wait for some sunny weather to see if sunlight will help. Thankfully stains don’t affect function, just the aesthetics. Since I didn’t spend more than $5 per diaper-insert set, I’m also not too concerned about needing to sell them again after we no longer use them so the stains aren’t a big deal.
