Thankfully my sister’s kids are the same size, so I was able to limit my luggage to just a carry-on suitcase, a backpack (with snacks and activities), one car seat, and a diaper bag. I didn’t have to take a car seat with me, but I decided that having one on board would be safer and better overall, so I had to find a way to lug it without needing to carry it.

The suitcase stroller

I looked online for ideas, and saw a couple of products to help mitigate this problem – one was a foldable trolley where you put the car seat (and child in) and the other was a strap that looked like a T where it attached the car seat to a carry-on suitcase. Well, I didn’t want to buy anything that was only useful in this situation or bring along anything extra (like the trolley), so I tried to use what I had to mimic the T-strap. A mom in a local mom’s Facebook group asked me to post some screens of my setup when she saw a photo of what I did, and j.w wanted to record a video of it, so he did a wonderful job of capturing my explanation, Bean and all: [embed]https://youtu.be/zbpq4FggdOs[/embed] For those who don’t want to watch the unedited video, read on for some written instructions.

Materials

[caption id="attachment_6504" align="alignright" width="225"] What you need before connecting everything together[/caption] This is what I used – and what you might need:
  • An adjustable luggage strap. You might be able to get away without this if the LATCH straps on your car seat are long enough to wrap around to the back of the suitcase; mine cannot. It seems that Asian families back in the day were known to wrap their suitcases with luggage straps (to keep it together and to see it easier on the carousel, maybe?), so it was quite easy for me to find one lying around. I like this particular one because there is a loop in it that the carabiner clips in so it’s snug and it doesn’t move. I’ve also used a bungee cord in a pinch, when I left my luggage strap behind – but I didn’t feel it was safe to put a child in it then.
  • One carabiner. If it’s weight supported (such as ones meant for climbing), it’s probably safer; mine’s not.
  • A key ring, O-ring, or D-ring. You might be able to get away without this if the top tether can clip to the carabiner, but I find it much easier with it. Probably safer if it’s weight-tested as well. If you get rid of your luggage strap, you would need this so that the LATCH clips can clip/hook onto the ring.

The setup

[caption id="attachment_6503" align="alignright" width="225"] A closer look at what it looks like after everything is connected together[/caption] A three-step summary:
  1. Match the back of the car seat against the front of the carry-on suitcase when vertically standing
  2. Thread the luggage strap through the car seat where the seat belt would go for a forward-facing set up, and adjust so that car seat is snug against the suitcase. If there are handles on your suitcase, put the luggage strap through them (the video shows the straps are twisted – try to make sure they’re flat before continuing).
  3. Take the top tether of the car seat and thread it through the handles on top of the suitcase and the handle bar (I pull up the handlebar to make it easier), and hook and clip the tether to the ring, or the carabiner, if you don’t use a ring. Tighten, tilt, and test it out.
With the car seat strapped to the carry-on suitcase, I could either put all my stuff into the seat while Bean helped roll it, or she sat in it and helped hold some stuff, while I wore my 18 month old in a carrier. People looked our way, but I’m not sure if it’s because people haven’t seen others do this, and/or they were thinking what kind of crazy parent would do that. On Air Canada (Dreamliner specifically), I can roll this contraption down the aisle and get to my seat without undoing anything, but it does not fit down the aisle on WestJet or Air Canada Rouge planes – some will fit down business class but once you get to economy, you will quickly find out it doesn’t fit so it’s better just to undo it in the area where parents gate check their strollers and car seats. You will also need to buffer a little more time to take it apart and put it together again as you go through security screening. Happy travelling!]]>

Even with help, I’m feeling drained and spent; I don’t know how single parents or those whose spouses are away do it – kudos to them. One huge advantage of the postpartum confinement practice is the help: I can get away from not having to do dishes, cook meals, change as many diapers, and entertain the toddler so I can feed the baby on demand,  get some shut-eye to make up for the little sleep I get at night. When help – in the form of either set of parents – leaves, I worry most about how to juggle the sleep schedules with two (bad parenting habits on our part as some would say, as the toddler requires too much help to fall asleep). I also hope I can figure out a way to connect with my daughter again because right now, I feel like mommy doesn’t have fun with her anymore; instead, mommy is just around to help her do things (like change diapers, feed her, bedtime routine, take her to the toilet, wash her hands) – which is probably why she’s even more resistant to other people helping her do these things these days, and would on occasion breakdown or have an outburst as a result. Lately, a typical day looks like: feed baby, change diaper, eat a meal, feed toddler, feed baby, put toddler down for a nap (short bedtime routine), power nap or laundry, feed baby, tend to toddler who has woken up within the hour, change diapers on both, eat another meal, feed toddler, feed baby, persuade the toddler to fall sleep (long bedtime routine), feed baby, some down time and snacks, and repeat the following until the morning: feed baby, nap, feed baby, change diaper, feed baby, settle toddler. (green: baby, orange: me, pink: toddler) Nights are the hardest because when I’m not sleeping, I silently will my husband to share in misery instead of serenely sleeping away or wonder why my toddler can’t easily be consoled by daddy when she wakes up in the middle of the night (and decides to cry for an hour instead).  I know these aren’t very nice (or godly) thoughts and I know it gets better eventually, but it doesn’t mean that they escape me. There has been a week of reprieve, where he actually followed a nice 2-3 hour feeding schedule, and slept nicely in between. Sadly, it didn’t last and was too good to be true.  At the time I started writing this post, he decided to feed every 1-2 hours nightly for a few days likely due to a growth spurt, but last night, it was nice to be back on a 2-3 hour sleep cycle again (funny how 2-3 hours is what to look forward these days). Though, it’s nice to have caught up with my Netflix playlist as a result. The past few weeks have gone by quickly in terms of baby’s growth: he lost 10% of his birth weight, but recovered 1 kg within a week. Perhaps it’s because the midwife wisely waited until he grew a bit before doing the heel poke for the newborn tests, but he didn’t even flinch or cry when she drew blood. While he easily tucked into my arms before, his feet now stick out when he’s feeding. He gurgles and snorts and makes all sorts of funny expressions and noises, and while he slept through all sorts of background noises the second week, it seems like he’s starting to want to be held more during the day. He’s also prone to burp and regularly spit up (even from his nose, poor guy), which I guess makes up for all the two times Bean did. This is why babies are hard work! I’m reminded why I started to enjoy being with and interacting with Bean more after she was 6 months old – by then, I probably would have a better hang of things with two as well.]]>
