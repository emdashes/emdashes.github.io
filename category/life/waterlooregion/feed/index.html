Originally posted: October 22, 2010Â | Revisited today, as we’re usingÂ mobile more than ever before.
    uxWaterloo is a local group that meets regularly with folks interested in the user experience of products and services. This month, James Wu, a UI Architect, spoke at the meeting about design considerations for mobile experiences.Â This event particularly interested me, not only because of the topic, but particularly also because the guest speaker is from Kobo, a CanadianÂ (at the time) e-book company (that integrates with Pocket, so you can download and read everything you’ve saved online offline and without the ads!).
    Like many, I have traditionally focused more on web desktop experiences, but I have on occasion needed to design for a mobile experience as well. James eloquently summed up the challenges I have often come across when transitioning to the mobile space, and reminded me of others that aren’t always thought of, such as:

    Screen Real Estate

    • Screen size (diagonal space)
      For example, a 29″ desktop monitor could be 300-500% bigger than a mobile phone
    • Orientation – the changes from portrait and landscape (and vice versa)
      What’s interesting is that during their user research with 1024×600 tablets (such as the Samsung tablet), they observed that users prefered using this size of tablets in landscape format
    • Screen resolution
      Using the same 1024×600 example, you would either have your screen in “short and wide” format, or “narrow and long”
    • On screen keyboard
      On many touch devices, more than 1/2 the screen is taken up by the keyboard, which would cover anything underneath it
    • Platform variationsÂ (orÂ device / hardware variations)
      The takeaway? Design for different experiences/products; don’t cram everything into one screen if it’s not necessary. Why? “Don’t necessarily think people use their phones the same way as tablets [desktops, etc.].” James said that at Kobo, they must design knowing that people will have multiple devices; the experience should be consistent across them, but not [necessarily] identical.

    Hardware

    Like desktop computers, hardware varies across different models. For the mobile devices, things like processor and memory are important to consider as it could affect battery life, performance, and even result in side effects like heat. Others include:

    • Removable storage: the ability to “hot swap” a media card while the application is running in either the background or the foreground could cause undesired effects if designed improperly
    • Interaction mechanisms: these may effect how a user interacts with an application such as trackballs, trackpads, keyboards, gesture strips (like the Palm Pre)

    Gestures
    For touch devices, we get into the a realm that is still pretty new. There is not yet official standards for touch gestures and there is limited documentation to illustrate them (James suggested that Apple may currently have the leading edge).

    • Core/standard gestures like tap, swipe, pinch, expand, drag
      For example, a more common gesture is the Pinch-Zoom to adjust the font size of Apple devices
    • Peripheral gestures may include less familiar gestures such as long press, rotate, shake, sequences
      Using Apple again for this example, a less common might be shaking the iPhone to perform the undo action

    When designing for touch, one must consider gestures could be used in different directions and even with multiple fingers. It isn’t surprising that some devices even recognize simultaneous touches (imagine designing a piano keyboard app so that multiple keys could be played at once to create a chord).
    It’s also important to provide users with affordances that let them know that features are there, and that they will use. Kobo uses instructional overlays for first-time use, as well as ghosted elements. I tried to imagine what a “ghosted element” was based on James’ description and thought of the many mobileÂ gamesÂ where they use these visual cuesÂ to indicate the controls (where to tap on the screen) as a part of the onboarding experience.
    Kobo may also add advanced gestures for more advanced users (like a keyboard shortcut on the desktop), such as adding an element of fun by adding a gestural easter egg in the Kobo app.

    User Context

    We must remember that users are likely in different environments and may have more constraints than if they’re sitting in front of a desktop. Considerations include:

    • Mobility: device may not be plugged in (may impact battery life for example)
    • Divided attention: users could be multi-tasking or looking at the device on the go
      [e.g. using their devices while walking from the train platform to catch the connecting train]
    • Unpredictable surroundings: things like flakey connections [e.g. online then loses reception while train goes under the tunnel] could impact a download or chat experience
    • Cost: How much would it cost by running your application? Unfortunately, not everyone has unlimited data. Some customers pay per byte, and that might mean customizing for wifi vs. data
    • Others: the amount of light (dark environment) or even the number of free hands could impact how they interact with the device.

    I’ve observed commuters carrying a briefcase in one hand, newspapers in another, and managed to hold a cup a coffee, along with their device. In that case, could they still perform a multi-touch gesture, and would they still be able to navigate even if they couldn’t?
    Knowing what users are doing while they’re using your product is a good advantage. Lesson from Kobo: James told us of a time when they hid the notification bar, including the time, to minimize distractions while the user was reading. Yet, it didn’t occur to them that if a customer didn’t have a watch or an easy way to check for time, they had to exit out of the application. Now, the notification bar can be brought back with a gesture.

    Standards and Expectations

    Customers might expect applications to be specific to their product. When Kobo designed an app that looked like it was for Apple device, Android customers weren’t happy. But once they took away the “Apple” button and changed the color, they noticed a decrease in complaints about the look of their application.
    So when designing for a mobile experience…
    …these are just some considerations and potential challenges that may come up while designing for a mobile product. Others are shared across the desktop too. We can’t forget who we’re designing for, the primary user for your product. For Kobo [at the time], it’s for women in the mid 30-40s, simply because they found that they spent the most money. Who knows, you might even need to take accessibility concerns as a requirement, simply because it’s the law.

    Starter Brew

    Fully brewed kombucha should smell like vinegar (or a faint smell if it’s not too strong), and a formula for starter brew comprises of: 1-2 cups of full brewed kombucha (the starter liquid) + SCOBY that is at least 1/8 inch thick (5oz) and should cover at least 1/2 of the surface area / opening of the jar/container/vessel, which is added toÂ 1 gallon brewed tea; the more SCOBY you have, the more likely you will succeed.     SCOBY

    What’s a SCOBY? It stands for Symbiotic Culture of Bacteria and Yeast.

    • A healthy one can look like a thin puck andÂ it’s not unusual to see spits, holes, strands, floaties and strings of yeast[ref]edible, but most people filter it out[/ref]. When you pick it up, it should stay together and shouldn’t tear easily. A new SCOBY is beige, creamy in colour; the underside will likely look more weird than the top side, and colours of blue or purple aren’t unusual; it might sink, and that’s okay. If your container is too small for the SCOBY, it is safe to cut it up.
    • An older SCOBY can turn brown, work slower or stop working, but you can just keep it there in the batch. However, if you see mold on it, just scrap it and start new. Â  Signs of mold include powdery, dry splotches, specks in black, blue, or white; people have compared it to mold on an orange or a piece of bread.

    • Making an ideal brew: it thrives in 73-85F temperature, out of direct sunlight and avoid an exterior wall if possible.     • Food-grade plastic, glass, or stainless steel is okay, despite what online research mayÂ say

    Brewing SweetÂ Tea

    1. Water

      • Must be chlorine-free, clean, potable
      • Avoid minerals – hard water can slow it down or kill it so if you’re using tap, boil it first and then let it sit overnight; then reboil it for tea

    2. Sugar

      • 1 cup sugar per 4L / 1 gallon of tea / kombucha. Don’t worry, 50-80% of the sugar gets converted
      • White is good; evaporated cane juice crystals is the best choice. A bag of Kirkland organic cane sugar is supposedly only $4!
      • If you decided to use honey, he recommends that you getÂ the pasteurized kind because the raw, unpasteurized kind contains anti-bacterial properties, thus may kill it[ref]Jun, is a honey-based kombucha, and using raw honey seems to yield success[/ref].       • Do not use artificial or plant sugar (e.g. stevia, agave), as it won’t work

    3. Tea

      • Black tea is the best; use bagged or loose
      • Avoid flavoured or herbal teas; any natural oils in them can kill the SCOBY (makes sense since some essential oils have anti-fungal, anti-bacterial properties)
      • Should just stay “tea” in the ingredients; decaf is okay
      • BoilÂ 4-5 bags of teaÂ in aÂ 2 gallon pot of water for 15+ minutes; cool it down to lower than body temperature before adding it to the starter brew

    Ready State

    • Is the kombucha ready to drink?Â You can use a straw (and a finger to suck up some tea) for the taste test, or more scientifically, you can also use PH strips to check for the ideal 3.5 PH; a PH of 4.0 is indicative of a new batch, <3.0 is more potent.     When washing the jars and other equipment for kombucha, don’t use antibacterial soap, including on your hand; you can easily use hot water and vinegar to wash and rinse.

    2. Use liquid nitrogen to burn it off. I asked how the pain would be compared to a needle, and she said it would probably be the same intensity but longer.
    3. Something similar to #2 but with local freezing. We opted to go with this.


    For whatever reason, she decided to take a second look, and this time, IT MOVED. I appreciate that she didn’t freak out, because I would have – and certainly did inside. Instead, she calmly said that she was going outside to get a second doctor’s opinion and help. The second doctor was a lot more certain that it was a tick and they partnered to quickly remove it with a pair of tweezers; it was still very much alive in the urine sample jar they put it in, so we assume that every bit came out (thus reducing Lyme disease, if at risk) – the second doctor must have really wanted to make sure for he must have pulled extra skin and hair off, because Bean started crying even more and I was handed a piece of gauze to apply pressure to stop the bleeding[ref]a week later, the spot looks raised with a dark scab, and it must feel tender because it still bothers her[/ref]. Google helped identify the tick as a dog tick, which isn’t as great of a threat as the black-legged ticks, but they jarred it anyway to be sent for analysis. She called a contact to see if we needed to start antibiotic treatment, as their guidelines only offered advice to those over 8 years of age. Later, she also followed up to tell us that Public Health would call if they found something of concern and texted us some close-up photos. We don’t have a dog, but we did go to the urban natural area the day before, and that’s likely where we picked it up.
    I also appreciated that she apologized and retracted her first diagnosis; she had seen ticks before, but none this size[ref]I don’t blame her – Google tells me that it was an adult female in its partially fed state so it would be bigger[/ref]. She’s a family doctor, so I can’t expect her to know everything, even though we hope they know what ails us. She recognized that even though I had made comments about characteristics reminding me of an insect, it was likely that everyone’s quick glance assumptions as a skin tag threw everyone off.

    I like being outdoors. I enjoy aspects of camping, canoeing, and especially hiking; I remember my dad would take us on walks and bike rides around trails when we were much younger. In all my years doing activities outdoors, I would have never imagined having to deal with this. Thankfully, I think this experience was more traumatic for me than it was for Bean, since she didn’t see what was bothering her, despite some tears and pain.
    Thank God that Bean noticed it, and He answered our prayers, giving wisdom to our doctor who for reason decided to do a second  look – because who knows if we would’ve found it otherwise. Plus, I am really glad that I didn’t have to remove it myself.

This isn’t the first time someone has asked for ideas, so I figured a compiled list of what I follow may be helpful to my other self and for future reference – and even though I don’t necessarily have time do venture out to many events offered around time, I still try sometimes! So what piques my interest? Generally, I like to know about free/low-cost activities that don’t need too much commitment.
     
    Here are some of my sources:
    • Meetup groups – a few that I subscribe to, include:
      • Badminton Fun in KW – John sends out a reminder of places that offer drop-in badminton around town (mostly at community centres)
      • Tri-Cities Food Lovers – I tend to follow this group for restaurant ideas (haven’t been to a meetup yet)
      • It’s On – Events for 20s and 30s – a central place for all the events that Kitchener Public Library offers for a specific crowd, though open to all.       • Hiking for Beginners – while the group says Cambridge, Ontario, I find that a bunch of the hikes are actually in the Kitchener area. I tend to follow them to discover trails around me as the timing for the meetups hasn’t been right for me yet.
      • I also follow the Chinese-English Social Cultural Exchange meetup group to keep a tabs on Chinese eating places around town
    • Public Library events
      • I tend to find out about interesting events from the library posters, TV screens, and their newsletters; e.g. Etc., Music Notes, KPL News. Popular events that have limited seats tend to fill quickly, and if you end up registering late, you’ll be added to a waitlist. A few that I’ve attended include one on coffee, making your own terrarium during #diyfestival,  interior design, and an upcoming ones that seem interesting is an art class, an escape room adventure featured for international games day, and a DIY string art piece.
      • You can also filter events in the calendar to browse for ones that interest you (hint: set filters by Date: today’s date, leave end date open; Event type: Adult, Arts & Culture, Business; Age Group: Adults – List view). Notably, KPL Central branch offers a weekly free movie screening in their theatre space.
    • Community Centres / Community Associations tend to offer many classes at a reasonable cost, but they usually require registration and a 8-10 weeks of commitment. I find that I have to browse through the leisure guides physically because the online versions aren’t very browseable (ref. Waterloo / Kitchener). I’ve noticed that similar classes tend to vary in price depending on where it’s offered, perhaps because of each community centre’s surrounding demographics (e.g. Downtown Kitchener seems to have lower drop-in prices than say RIM Park in Waterloo)
      • Cooking classes
      • Art classes (e.g. $5 a class for drawing)
      • Language classes (e.g. ASL – $5+ /class, Spanish, Conversational Chinese – $5 a class on Saturday mornings)
      • Social Dance / Ballroom dancing classes, even more affordable than the ones taught at University of Waterloo (outside of the clubs)
      • Drop-in sport activities – organized and self-organized, such as badminton (from $3 a session)
    • City of Kitchener and City of Waterloo newsletters, which include a Kitchener Market sub-newsletter
      • By extension, the Downtown Kitchener BIA also offers a e-newsletter that highlights events such as NightShift and Art Market, and the annual Art Walk (local artisans featuring their works for sale)
    • Free community skating / swimming / aqua-fit sessions
    • Theatre:
      • KW Little Theatre – some nights by donation or pay-what-you-can
      • Arts Alumni Theatre Night – free, registration required – productions by current students at UW
      • Lost and Found Theatre – free nights 
    • Free academic / public lectures, for the geek-nerds in us, such as:
      • PI Institute – I have attended a couple in the past, one on music and physics
      • University of Waterloo Public Lectures (and any of its affiliated colleges)
    • Miscellaneous:
      • I really enjoyed the events that Clipped Outdoors offered for women, but they haven’t been active in a while so I wonder what happened…
      • Community events offered by local churches (e.g. canning, community gardening) – I find out about them usually from their signs as a passerby
