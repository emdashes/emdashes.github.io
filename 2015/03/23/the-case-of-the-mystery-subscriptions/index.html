The Case of the Mystery Subscriptions

Posted byEmily March 23, 2015 1 Comment on The Case of the Mystery Subscriptions

Edward Snowden was, which reminded me of this post that’s been sitting in draft for a while now, loosely about the end of our privacy. We’re online and we give data (and our identity) away willingly for free. For example, in exchange for free services that we use, Google (and likely Facebook) knows where we live (ever searched using Google Maps using your address as a starting point or uploaded geo-tagged photos), what we and our families look like (photo tags from you or your friends), our phone number (optional but persistent security question for Google accounts), and if you’ve ever sent or received sensitive information using your gmail address, they probably have your SIN or credit card information as well; they probably also have a good idea of whether you’re healthy (based on what you search for), who you know (based on who you email often or who your friends are), and whether you’re home (have a Nest?).  So, I guess by being online, there’s an assumed risk that we have to take and sometimes, you end up with a story like the one below.

Shortly after watching Sherlock’s latest episode, we encountered our very own mystery: one of us had started receiving unsolicited marketing emails from clothing and body-building retailers and other non-profit organizations (that seem to bear no relevance to each other such as Canadian Nurses Association and Communist Party of  Canada – I had thought maybe the culprit was trying to suggest that one of us needed a wardrobe update until I came across these).  It probably would’ve been less suspicious if it had occurred over a larger span of time as I get random email subscriptions sometimes (by people who have made a typo or want to use a dummy email address), but the large influx occurred over 8 hours: 38 to be exact, and 26 in under 1 hour. Likely because of Sherlock’s influence, the detective in me started looking for clues to the culprit and realized a few things:
  • A couple of retailers may have requested basic profile information prior to creating an account, which means this person must have spent time to sign up each mailer individually and manually – at first, I thought maybe it was a joke or prank, but then realized the effort involved*…what kind of person has that much time on their hands? Instead of using dummy data, some slip-ups that culprit left behind included using our address with their postal code belonging to their place of work
    • This also meant that we either knew the culprit or (s)he was a stalker and knew exactly where we lived (not sure which one was the better evil at the time: a frienemy or a creeper?)
  • Of the 38 sign ups, 3 different email addresses were used. My guess it that this person must not have known that one of them is hardly used, so it helped narrow down our guesses on who it might have been based on the address/postal code used and who we know has all 3 email addresses (and then the motive or why).
  • Turns out that it’s not easy to trace the path of an email subscriber. Bigger companies will not release IP addresses of the original subscriber unless legally required to do so and without that, you’re left guessing. Amazingly, one smaller company, with a less professional email template actually included IP address information at the bottom of an email (so emails that look like spam can actually turn out to be useful)! Thankfully, this person decided to do everything from a static IP address that wasn’t tied to a generic internet provider.
Well, well, well! With that last tidbit, I applied what I learned from my friends in first year to trace back the source (who knew that those tricks would actually become useful!), et voilà, we got to the bottom of it in no time! *Unfortunately, to this day, the culprit has not ‘fessed up to anything.
I guess that story had a better ending than the victims of the creepy people (popular on Instagram) who claim other people’s baby photos as their own children…]]>
