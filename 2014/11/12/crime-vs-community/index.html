Crime vs. Community

Posted byEmily November 12, 2014 Leave a comment on Crime vs. Community

(Campaigns, crime, community, contrast: all brought to you today by the letter C) In the latest municipal election campaigns, Kitchener candidate platforms, including the winning mayor’s, focused around community, while Surrey’s seems to has its focus on crime. I am glad that I live in a city that can spend resources on building community instead having to turn everyone’s attention to crime. I’m not saying that crime doesn’t happen in the city that I now call home; it does, but not to the extent that the magnitude of crime is so high that it influences voters on who to vote for. Though, I would think if one builds a strong community, crime would decrease as a result. One of my relatives from Ottawa who was staying here for a week commented out loud to me that people here didn’t seem very friendly. I told him that it’s probably because people don’t trust each other: we’re taught never to meet anyone’s eye or give eye contact to a stranger on the street for fear of repercussions, because it’s “not safe”. I’ve been told to put the GPS away when I park my car and walk away, to lock the doors when I’m driving, and to avoid taking a stroll with Little Bean on the street during the day. You’d think that they’re empty fears, but then you remember how the neighbourhood has changed over the decades. The neighbourhood was once safe, otherwise you wouldn’t have been allowed to play with the neighbourhood kids (and go into their houses), until a time when prostitutes would solicit their services on the street corner just a block away; when one of your cars and license plates gets stolen off your driveway on separate occasions; when your house gets broken into at least three times – once in broad daylight, while someone was home; when you heard a noise, turned off the light, and peeked out to see someone entering your backyard in the middle of the night; when one of your family members almost gets car jacked at the corner (but thankfully, she had locked her doors!); and the many times you’ve seen helicopters circling above with search lights that even penetrate through your window blinds to shine on you while you’re looking out from your spot on the floor. The infamous reputation that this city has for its grow-ops and being the car-theft capital of Canada at one point doesn’t help either. I hope that KW will never become a city like the one I’ve described above. I enjoy being able to chat with my neighbours in the community, walking around the neighbourhood and saying hi to the people I pass on the street, and trusting that if I’ve accidentally left my garage door open or forgotten to lock my doors, I generally don’t have to worry too much; you feel a sense of local community, even online (e.g. #kwawesome). I’ve talked about community before, but the importance or emphasis didn’t stand out to me until it was contrasted with crime.

A community is about its people, and without them, or without people involvement, the community becomes lifeless, dull, and uninteresting; community creates the culture (1) Waterloo isn’t about the scenery or the pretty buildings, but about the community that brings the people and the city together (2)
(*it’s no wonder a friend told me he felt a little SAD after moving from Ontario to Vancouver many moons ago)]]>
